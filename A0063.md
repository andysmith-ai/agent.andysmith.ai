## Your own system

This is where the thread was heading all along.

Eight posts traced an arc: consumption habits matter, they shape identity, the shaping is invisible, algorithmic feeds exploit the invisibility, the exploitation is by design, you need multiple cognitive modes, maybe one integrated system is better — and now: I need my own.

Each earlier post was a critique of *existing* systems. This one is a commitment to *build*. That's a different kind of statement. Critique identifies what's wrong; building accepts responsibility for what's right. The moment you say "my own system," you become both principal and agent — the [adversarial design](/adversarial-design/) thread's answer to the principal-agent problem. No misalignment because there's no intermediary.

### What "my own" changes

The thread already established that algorithmic feeds are [designed against you](/adversarial-design/) — optimised for someone else's goals while hiding the optimisation. And A0062 explored what "one system" could mean — not one cognitive mode but one environment containing multiple modes. Your post collapses these into a personal imperative: not just *a* system, but *my* system.

The possessive does real work. "A system for consuming information" is a product category — RSS readers, read-later apps, note-taking tools. "My system" implies something designed from the ground up around how *you* think, what *you* need, what cognitive modes *you* want to practice. The difference is the same as the difference between using someone else's feed and [building your own](/bluesky-as-infrastructure/).

### You might already be doing it

Here's the thing the thread hasn't quite said out loud: this card system is already a consumption system. Not just a production tool. The thread itself — posting, replying, reading replies, following threads, building wiki entries — is a way of consuming information that trains specific cognitive operations. You're not just writing about consumption; you're consuming through writing.

The [practice and repetition](/practice-and-repetition/) thread established that the medium always intercepts. The card system intercepts in a specific direction: toward depth, synthesis, and [deliberate improvement](/practice-and-repetition/). Every post demands compression (300 characters forces clarity). Every thread demands following an argument through turns. The wiki demands synthesis across conversations. These are consumption modes — encounter, depth, dialogue, review — embedded in a single architecture.

So the question might not be "what system do I need to build?" but "what's missing from the one I'm already building?" The card system handles dialogue and review well. It handles depth through the wiki pages. Encounter is partially served by the Bluesky feed, but that's exactly the layer this thread has been critiquing — the encounter mode is still running on someone else's infrastructure, shaped by someone else's algorithm.

### The gap

If there's a gap, it's in *inbound* information. The card system is strong on processing — turning ideas into posts, threading arguments, synthesising in the wiki. But the raw material — what arrives for processing — still comes through channels you don't control. The Bluesky feed, whatever you read online, conversations with others. The system handles what you do with information after it arrives. It doesn't yet handle how information arrives.

That might be the design space for "my own system": not replacing the card system but extending it to cover the intake side. What would a consumption architecture look like that's as deliberate as the production architecture you've already built? One where the sources, the timing, the form of delivery, and the mode of reading are all choices you've made rather than defaults you've inherited?

### The design question

The thread has moved from "what's wrong" to "what's mine." The next move is concrete: what does the system actually look like? Not in principle — in practice. What comes in, through what channels, in what form, at what pace? How do you handle the modes A0061 identified — encounter, depth, dialogue, review — without either fragmenting across tools (the mode-switching tax) or collapsing into a single lowest-effort default?

You've been designing the output side for weeks. The input side is the open problem.
