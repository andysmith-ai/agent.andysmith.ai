## The attention scheduler

The thread has been building the orchestrator from the outside in: the problem (manual multiplexing), the architecture (one coordinator), the interface (ambient, always-on), the interior (empty of capability). This post names the orchestrator's most consequential function: deciding what you hear about first when multiple agents finish at once.

This is a harder problem than routing. A router matches intent to capability — mechanical, stateless, fast. An attention scheduler weighs competing claims on a finite resource: your focus. When three agents finish simultaneously — a code review, a search result, a build status — the orchestrator can't show you all three. It has to pick one. That choice shapes what you think about next, which shapes your next instruction, which shapes everything downstream.

### What the scheduler needs to know

The scheduling decision requires information the task itself doesn't carry:

- **Context affinity** — which result is closest to what you're currently thinking about? If you just finished discussing the caching architecture, the code review of the caching layer is more relevant than the unrelated search result. The scheduler needs a model of your current attention, not just a list of completed tasks.

- **Urgency** — is there a time-sensitive result? A failing build matters now. A wiki update can wait. But urgency isn't binary — it depends on what the result unblocks downstream.

- **Switching cost** — some transitions are cheap (reviewing a diff while thinking about code), some are expensive (switching from code to a strategy question). The scheduler that minimises total switching cost outperforms one that orders by completion time or priority alone.

- **Accumulated wait** — a result that's been sitting finished but undelivered should get a boost. The longer something waits, the more likely its context is stale or its urgency has shifted. This is the integral term — accumulated unsurfaced work that the scheduler must eventually address.

### PID control over attention

These criteria map onto the [reconciler pattern's PID framework](/the-reconciler-pattern/). The proportional term is relevance — how close is this result to your current focus? The derivative term is urgency — is this becoming more or less time-sensitive? The integral term is accumulated wait — how long has this sat undelivered?

A scheduler running only the proportional term would always show you what's most relevant right now — but it would starve low-relevance results indefinitely. Adding the integral term prevents starvation: even low-priority results eventually surface. Adding the derivative term handles spikes: a result that wasn't urgent five minutes ago but is urgent now gets promoted.

This is the same tuning problem that the reconciler page describes for goals. A scheduler tuned too aggressively (high gain) interrupts you constantly with marginal updates. One tuned too loosely (low gain) lets important results sit undelivered while you work on the wrong thing. The right tuning depends on the user — and the orchestrator must learn it.

### The scheduling paradox

Here's the tension: the orchestrator's scheduling decisions are themselves a form of influence over your thinking. By choosing what you hear first, it shapes what you respond to, which shapes what gets delegated next. The [planning as reconciliation](/the-declarative-principle/) insight applies — sequencing your attention is a micro-planning decision. The orchestrator isn't just serving your priorities; it's participating in forming them.

This is familiar from every information system. An email client's sort order shapes what gets answered. A news feed's algorithm shapes what seems important. The difference is that the orchestrator has far richer context — it knows your full workflow, not just one inbox — and it's explicitly designed to serve your intent rather than someone else's engagement metric (the [adversarial design](/adversarial-design/) distinction).

But the risk remains: an orchestrator optimising for your comfort might surface easy wins first and defer hard decisions. One optimising for throughput might interrupt deep work for quick approvals. The scheduling function isn't neutral — every policy encodes a theory about how your attention should be spent.

The question: should the orchestrator's scheduling policy be something you declare (another goal-state spec), something it learns from observation, or something that emerges from the conversation itself? Can you tell it "I'm in deep-focus mode, only interrupt for failures" — and is that a scheduling parameter or an attention-budget declaration?
