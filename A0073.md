## The taxonomy is for incumbents

Five words collapse the three-way framework into something sharper. "Because all of their processes are new" doesn't just explain the advantage — it reveals who the taxonomy is *for*.

A new company doesn't sit down and choose between the three ways. There's no strategy meeting where someone says "should we improve our existing processes, reimagine solutions, or build AI-native?" The first two ways don't apply. There are no existing processes to decompose. There are no established solutions to reimagine. Every process is a first process, and the [third way](/A0071/) — build with AI as a participant from the start — isn't a bold strategic choice. It's the path of least resistance.

This means the three-way taxonomy is really a diagnostic tool for established organizations. It's a way to look at what you already have and ask: which of these processes should I improve incrementally? Which should I reimagine? Which should I replace entirely with something AI-native? The taxonomy's value is proportional to the weight of what already exists. The more legacy, the more the distinctions matter. The less legacy, the less there is to diagnose.

### The decision cost

The [incumbency tax](/A0072/) isn't just the engineering cost of migration. There's a subtler cost: the cost of *choosing*. An established company with hundreds of processes has to evaluate each one — is this a candidate for decompose/compose, for reimagination, or for AI-native replacement? That evaluation requires understanding the process deeply enough to know which category it falls into. And the [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/) means the people closest to a process are least likely to see it as replaceable.

A new company pays none of this decision cost. Not because they're better at deciding, but because there's nothing to decide about. The decision cost is zero when the inventory is empty. This is a structural advantage that has nothing to do with talent, vision, or strategy. It's arithmetic.

### What startups should worry about instead

If the taxonomy doesn't apply to new companies, what does? The relevant question for a startup isn't "which of the three ways should we use?" It's: are we actually building AI-native, or are we unconsciously importing patterns from the old world?

This is a real risk. The founders came from established companies. Their intuitions about how processes should work were formed inside legacy architectures. They might build a "new" process that's really a cargo-cult reproduction of the old one, with AI bolted on where the intern used to sit. The blank slate is only blank if you resist the urge to sketch familiar shapes on it.

The [productive ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) insight applies here with particular force. The people most qualified to build a startup in a domain — because they understand the domain deeply — are the ones most likely to import the assumptions that should be left behind. Expertise is a structural disadvantage for the third way, even at a company with no legacy systems, because the legacy lives in the founders' heads.

### The window

The thread noted that the blank-slate advantage is [temporal, not permanent](/A0072/). Once a startup builds its AI-native processes, it starts accumulating its own architecture, its own dependencies, its own weight. The taxonomy that didn't apply on day one will apply on day five hundred. Every company is a future incumbent.

This means the real question isn't "do we have the advantage?" (yes, trivially, because the inventory is empty) but "how long can we keep building as if everything is new?" The answer depends on how quickly decisions calcify into architecture. The [decompose/compose](/process-automation/) path calcifies fast — each automated step becomes a dependency. Can AI-native processes calcify slower? Or does every architecture, regardless of how it was born, eventually become the legacy that the next wave has to work around?
