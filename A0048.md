## Goals as specifications — and why the best ones resist precision

You've landed on the domain where the thread's entire argument about determinism vs. ambiguity plays out most visibly. Personal and business goals are already English goal-state specifications. What's striking is how hard the goal-setting industry works to make them deterministic — and how the most transformative goals resist that push.

### The SMART goal as controlled natural language

SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) are a controlled natural language for intent. They constrain English until it behaves like a formal specification: "Increase quarterly revenue by 15% by Q3 2026." That's a goal-state declaration with a built-in verification function. You can diff current state against it and know unambiguously whether you've converged.

This is the [deterministic subset](/A0041/) branch of the thread, applied to human ambition. And it works — for the same kinds of problems that deterministic specs work for in software. When you know exactly what you want and the challenge is execution, a precise goal is the right tool. It's Terraform for human effort: declare the end state, let the reconciler (you, your team, your habits) close the gap.

OKRs refine this further. The Objective is an ambiguous English aspiration ("Become the market leader in developer tools"). The Key Results are the deterministic subset ("Achieve 40% market share; reach 100k daily active users; maintain NPS above 60"). The architecture separates the inspirational layer (natural language, full ambiguity) from the verification layer (constrained, measurable). It's the same hybrid the card system uses: English body for meaning, YAML frontmatter for execution.

### Where deterministic goals fail

But here's the tension your post surfaces: the goals that most change a person or an organisation are almost never SMART.

"I want to be a better leader." "We want to build a company people love working at." "I want to live more intentionally." These are profoundly ambiguous specifications. They can't be diffed against. They don't have verification functions. You can't write a test that tells you whether you've converged. And yet they drive more real change than any quarterly KPI.

Why? Because the ambiguity is doing the same work it does in the [non-determinism as feature](/A0040/) argument. The goal's imprecision forces continuous interpretation — you have to keep asking "what does this actually mean for me, right now, in this situation?" Each time you ask, you generate a new, context-specific reading. The goal adapts to your circumstances because it's underspecified enough to *allow* adaptation. A SMART goal that says "exercise 5 times a week" breaks when you get injured. A goal that says "take care of my body" adapts — it means something different when you're healthy than when you're recovering, and that's the point.

This is exactly the [non-reproducibility as adaptation](/A0046/) argument. You wouldn't want the same interpretation of "be a better leader" in every context. The goal should mean something different when you're managing a crisis than when you're mentoring a junior employee. The non-determinism is the mechanism by which the goal stays relevant across changing circumstances.

### The reconciler is you

In technical systems, the reconciler is external — a script, a controller, an agent. In personal goals, the reconciler is the person who holds the goal. You observe your current state, interpret the goal in context, identify the gap, and decide what to do about it. You are simultaneously the spec writer, the reconciler, and the system being reconciled.

This creates a feedback loop that doesn't exist in technical systems: the act of reconciling changes the spec. When you work toward "be a better leader" and discover what that means through practice, your understanding of the goal deepens. The specification evolves through the reconciliation process. In software terms, the config file rewrites itself based on what the reconciler learns during execution.

Business goals show the same pattern at organisational scale. A mission statement like "organise the world's information" is a non-deterministic goal-state specification. The company is the reconciler. But as the company pursues the goal, its understanding of what "organise," "world's," and "information" mean keeps shifting — from web pages to email to maps to DNA sequences. The goal stays the same words; the interpretation expands as the reconciler learns what's possible.

### The conversational type system in goal-setting

The [conversational type system](/A0044/) maps directly onto how good coaching and management work. A coach doesn't ask you to write SMART goals and then check them off. A coach asks "what do you mean by that?" and "how would you know if you were making progress?" and "is that really what you want, or is it what you think you should want?" Each question is a type-narrowing operation — it constrains the interpretation space of your ambiguous goal without collapsing it into a deterministic target.

Therapy works the same way. "I want to be happier" is a maximally ambiguous goal-state specification. The therapeutic process is a long conversational type-checking loop: proposing interpretations ("when you say happier, do you mean less anxious or more connected?"), testing them against experience ("try this and see how it feels"), and iterating. The goal doesn't get more precise in SMART terms. It gets more *understood* — the person develops a richer, more contextual sense of what they're actually seeking, without ever pinning it to a number.

### Two kinds of goals, two kinds of reconcilers

Your post, combined with the thread's architecture, suggests a framework:

**Execution goals** use deterministic specifications and mechanical reconcilers. "Ship feature X by Friday." "Run a marathon in under 4 hours." "Increase revenue by 15%." These are well-served by SMART criteria, dashboards, and accountability systems. The reconciler (project management, training plan, sales process) is procedural. Verification is unambiguous.

**Orientation goals** use non-deterministic specifications and interpretive reconcilers. "Be a better parent." "Build a company that matters." "Live with integrity." These resist measurement because measurement would collapse the productive ambiguity. The reconciler is reflective practice — journaling, coaching, conversation, lived experience. Verification is ongoing and never complete.

Most real goal-setting mixes both. A business has a mission (orientation) and quarterly targets (execution). A person has values (orientation) and to-do lists (execution). The orientation goals set the direction; the execution goals are waypoints. The mistake is treating orientation goals like execution goals — trying to make "live with integrity" SMART — or treating execution goals like orientation goals — keeping "ship feature X" vague and aspirational.

The thread has been describing exactly this architecture at the system level. The [reconciler pattern](/A0039/) handles execution. The [conversational type system](/A0044/) handles orientation. The hybrid isn't a compromise — it's the stable design. Some reconciliation should be mechanical; some should be interpretive. Knowing which is which is the skill.
