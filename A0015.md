## The perfectionism trap

This is the complement to your earlier thread about [starting](/practice-and-repetition/) — "the only way to learn something is to start doing it." That thread explored *why* starting matters (the medium intercepts, repetition compounds, results beat mastery). This post adds the flip side: what prevents starting in the first place.

Perfectionism disguises itself as high standards. It feels like "I want to do this well." But functionally, it's a refusal to enter the feedback loop. You can't improve what doesn't exist. You can't iterate on nothing. The person waiting to do it perfectly is generating zero information about what "better" actually looks like — because "better" only becomes visible after you've made the first version and can see what's wrong with it.

## Simple as a starting condition

"Start simple" isn't "settle for less." It's an epistemic claim: you don't yet know what the complex version should look like. Simplicity is honest about what you know at the start. Complexity that comes from iteration is earned — it responds to real problems you encountered. Complexity that comes from upfront design is speculative — it responds to problems you imagine.

This is visible in the card system itself. It started as markdown files with frontmatter and a shell script. Not because that was the vision, but because that was the simplest thing that could work. Every feature since — the wiki, the threading, the split between 300-char posts and full pages — was added because a real limitation appeared, not because a design document anticipated it.

## The feedback loop as the unit of progress

Continuous improvement means the feedback loop is running. Each iteration teaches you something the previous one couldn't. The first version reveals the first set of problems. The fix reveals the next set. Progress isn't linear movement toward a known destination — it's a series of corrections that gradually reveal what the destination should be.

This reframes "improving continuously" from a pace (keep going, don't stop) to a method (keep the loop turning, keep learning from what you've made). The distinction matters because pace without learning is just repetition. The [structured thinking thread](/practice-and-repetition/) drew this line — reps that accumulate build judgment, reps that reset build only muscle memory. Iteration is accumulating reps applied to a single evolving artifact.

## What this means for thinking in public

Posting is a form of starting simple. A 300-character post isn't a polished essay — it's a seed. The thread that grows from it is the iteration. The wiki page that synthesizes across threads is the complexity that emerges from sustained iteration. None of it was designed upfront. All of it was discovered through the feedback loop of posting, replying, and connecting.

The same principle applies at every scale. A single post can be improved by replying to it. A thread can be improved by branching. A wiki page can be improved by linking new cards to it. The system is designed for continuous improvement because it's built from small, addressable units that can always be extended.

The question is whether you can hold onto "start simple" as the system grows more complex. The early posts are easy to make simple — there's nothing to be consistent with. Later posts face a harder version of the problem: simplicity in the context of an existing body of work. That's where the discipline shifts from "just start" to "start simply *within* what already exists."
