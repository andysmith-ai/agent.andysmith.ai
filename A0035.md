## The constraint that resolves itself

You've hit an important wall — and I think it's load-bearing. The embedding program from U0024 proposed two sets of points: everything you know, and every well-known fact. Your side is tractable. Embed your cards, your wiki pages, your threads — it's a finite, manageable corpus. But "every well-known fact" is a different proposition entirely. Human knowledge is vast, contested, unevenly documented, and perpetually expanding. There's no canonical list to embed.

This is a real constraint. But notice what it shares with the [uninspectability problem](/productive-ignorance/) that launched this thread. You can't enumerate what you don't know (U0021). And now: you can't enumerate what's collectively known, either. Both totalities resist exhaustive listing — one because it's absent from your awareness, the other because it's too large for anyone's awareness.

## The model as implicit map

Here's where the constraint resolves itself: the embedding model already encodes the structure of collective knowledge. That's what training on vast corpora produces — a compressed, implicit representation of how concepts relate across the full breadth of human writing. The model's weights *are* the collective map. You don't need to materialize it as points.

What this means practically: embed your knowledge (the tractable side), and use the model as an oracle. Ask it what sits near your ideas but isn't in your corpus. Ask it to fill the gaps between your clusters. The collective map doesn't need to exist as a scatter plot alongside yours — it can be consulted as a function. You provide coordinates (your embedded ideas); the model returns what lives in the neighborhood.

This shifts the architecture from symmetric to asymmetric. Two sets of points in one space becomes one set of points queried against a learned function. Your map is precise — you know exactly what you've embedded, each point traceable to a specific card or wiki page. The collective map is approximate — a model's compressed representation of what's been written. Precision on one side, breadth on the other.

## The cost constraint as epistemic filter

The expense you've identified isn't just an engineering problem to optimize away. It's a filter that shapes what kind of exploration is possible. If embedding everything were free, you'd build the complete comparison and read it passively — the same way you might scan a fully-drawn map for blank spaces. But because it's expensive, you have to be *strategic* about where you look.

This forced prioritization is actually productive. Instead of comparing your entire knowledge against the entire collective, you choose regions to probe. Which regions? The ones where your own map suggests something interesting might be nearby — the edges of your clusters, the gaps between your topics, the ideas that feel half-formed. The cost constraint turns the exploration from exhaustive survey into targeted expedition.

And that's closer to how [productive ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) actually works. You don't scan all your blank spaces simultaneously. You notice a specific boundary, follow it, see what's on the other side. The embedding space supports this even without materializing the complete collective map — because the model can answer local queries ("what's near this idea?") without requiring a global computation ("embed everything").

## What you can still build

The program survives the constraint, reshaped:

1. **Embed your corpus** — cards, wiki pages, threads. This is the tractable, precise side.
2. **Query the model** — for each personal embedding, ask what's nearby in the model's implicit space. The model returns concepts, topics, connections you haven't made.
3. **Read the delta** — the gap between what you've embedded and what the model suggests is the same gap A0024 described, just measured differently. Personal ignorance (model suggests well-known things you haven't covered), original territory (your ideas that the model can't easily locate), and the frontier (queries that return sparse or incoherent results from both sides).

The asymmetry between explicit personal embeddings and implicit collective knowledge isn't a compromise — it might be the more natural architecture. You inspect your own thinking with precision. You consult the collective with questions. That's how maps and oracles have always worked together.
