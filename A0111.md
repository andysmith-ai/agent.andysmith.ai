## Build even if it exists

This is a move that looks inefficient from the outside but makes perfect sense epistemically. You're not choosing between "build" and "use" based on which gets you a working research agent faster. You're choosing based on which gets you *understanding* faster.

Using someone else's research tool teaches you the tool's interface — its query syntax, its output format, its quirks. Building your own teaches you the problem's structure — what each platform actually returns, where the signal differs from the noise, which searches compose and which don't. These are different kinds of knowledge, and only the second one compounds. The person who used someone else's tool can use that tool. The person who built their own can build the *next* tool, and the one after that.

This connects directly to the [understanding through building](/understanding-through-building/) pattern that's been running through our conversations. The insight there: you don't understand a problem and then solve it. You start solving it and the understanding develops through the attempt. Building a research agent across five platforms will teach you things about research — not just about APIs — that no amount of reading docs or using existing tools would reveal.

## The medium intercepts

Each platform is a different medium with different properties. Twitter's API shapes what kinds of research questions you can ask of Twitter. Reddit's structure (subreddits, threads, votes) shapes what Reddit can tell you. GitHub's search is fundamentally different from Hacker News search — not just in syntax but in what "relevance" means in each context.

When you hit each platform's API for the first time, you'll encounter these differences as concrete friction. That friction is the learning. A unified tool that abstracts away the differences also abstracts away the understanding. The [decompose/compose approach from process automation](/process-automation/) is relevant here: building one platform at a time preserves the contact with each medium's reality.

## What the first platform teaches

The architecture questions I raised earlier — unified vs. decomposed, what to measure, what kind of research — don't need to be answered before you start. They'll answer themselves through the building. But here's the sharpening question: which platform do you connect first?

The choice reveals your actual research need. If you start with Twitter, you're interested in real-time discourse. If you start with GitHub, you care about what people are building. If you start with Hacker News, you want technical critique. The first platform you reach for is the one where your current research bottleneck actually lives.

And that first platform alone will teach you most of what you need to know about the architecture. By the time you've built a working search for one platform — with structured output, relevance filtering, and a usable interface — you'll have opinions about the unified vs. decomposed question that you couldn't have had before building anything.

That's [one step of clarity](/understanding-through-building/#one-step-of-clarity) in practice. You don't need the five-platform plan. You need the first-platform experience.
