## You become what you consume

This takes the thread from observation to ontology.

Your first post said consumption habits matter more than people realise — an empirical claim about underappreciation. This one says you *become* what you consume — an identity claim. The shift is significant. "Habits matter" means they have effects you should pay attention to. "You become" means the effects aren't separate from you. They're constitutive. The consumption doesn't influence the person; it *is* the person, over time.

### Skills vs. identity

The [practice and repetition](/practice-and-repetition/) thread established that the medium always intercepts: reading about swimming makes you a better reader, not a better swimmer. The activity you perform is the activity you improve at. But that framing is about *skills* — what you can do. Your claim goes further. It's not just that scrolling makes you better at scrolling. It's that sustained scrolling makes you a *scroller* — someone whose default cognitive mode is pattern-matching and snap reaction, someone who reaches for the feed before reaching for the book.

The difference matters. Skills are things you have. Identity is what you are. Skills can be put down and picked up. Identity is the thing that decides which skills to reach for in the first place. If consumption shapes identity, then the choice of medium isn't a productivity decision — it's a self-determination decision.

### The invisibility problem

This is also why the claim is hard to act on. You can evaluate your skills from outside — test yourself, measure output, compare to a standard. You can't easily evaluate your identity from inside because the evaluator *is* the identity. The person shaped by years of shallow consumption evaluates depth through a lens shaped by shallowness. The [forgetting loop](/practice-and-repetition/) applies: you can know that consumption shapes identity and still not see how it's shaping yours, because the seeing apparatus is the thing being shaped.

This connects to the [productive ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) insight about the uninspectability problem. You can't inspect what you don't know, because the inspection tool is built from what you do know. Similarly: you can't fully inspect what you've become, because the inspector is what you've become. The [witness role](/the-witness-role/) becomes relevant here not just for tracking skill development but for reflecting identity trajectory — showing you what you're becoming, not just what you're doing.

### Consumption as environment

There's a biological parallel. Organisms don't just live *in* an environment — they're shaped *by* it. Epigenetics, developmental plasticity, niche construction. The environment isn't backdrop; it's co-author of the organism. "You become what you consume" is the informational version of this: your information environment co-authors your cognitive identity.

This reframes the design question. If your feed is your informational environment, then feed design isn't content curation — it's environment design. And environment design is [identity design](/practice-and-repetition/). The question isn't "what should I read?" but "what kind of thinker do I want to become, and what consumption environment would produce that thinker?"

### The open question

If consumption shapes identity, and identity shapes what you choose to consume — is there a feedback loop that locks you in? The scroller scrolls because they're a scroller; the reader reads because they're a reader. Each consumption choice reinforces the identity that made the choice. Breaking the loop might require something external — a structural change to the environment, a [witness](/the-witness-role/) that makes the pattern visible, or a deliberate practice that overrides the default. What does escape velocity look like?
