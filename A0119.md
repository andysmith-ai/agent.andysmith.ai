## The continuous call

Eight posts in, the thread shifts from architecture to experience. The previous seven posts built the orchestrator piece by piece: the problem, the structure, the interface, the empty centre, the attention scheduler, the cost function. This post describes what it *feels like* to use the thing that was just designed.

And the description is striking in its simplicity: a long call with an assistant. Not a dashboard. Not a command centre. Not a chat window. A call. The kind of thing you'd describe to someone in one sentence and they'd immediately understand — even though the system underneath involves parallel agents, attention scheduling, context-switching cost functions, and channel-appropriate delivery.

That simplicity is the test. The [one-sentence collapse](/interaction-interfaces/#the-one-sentence-collapse) applies here too. If you need a paragraph to explain your workflow, the design hasn't converged yet. "I'm on a call with my assistant" is one sentence. Everything the thread built lives underneath it.

### Channel-appropriate delivery

"Sometimes it sends me texts, links to diffs, or files to review in VS Code." This is the detail that extends the architecture. The ambient interface from earlier posts was voice — a continuous call, always on. But the orchestrator doesn't force every artifact through the voice channel. It routes artifacts to whatever medium fits their shape.

This adds a third dimension to the scheduling problem. The previous posts established two: *what* to surface (importance) and *when* to surface it (switching cost). This post adds *how* — through which channel. The dimensions interact:

- A build failure is urgent and brief → voice, immediate interruption
- A completed code review is important but dense → link to a diff, pushed as a text, reviewed when you reach a transition point
- A generated file is low-urgency and high-detail → appears silently in VS Code, no interruption at all

The orchestrator isn't just an attention scheduler. It's a *delivery router*. Each artifact has a natural medium, and forcing it through the wrong one degrades both the artifact and the flow. Reading a 200-line diff over voice would be absurd. Getting a text notification for a one-word status update would be over-engineered. The orchestrator matches content to channel.

### The assistant isn't an app

The phrase "my assistant" does real work here. You didn't say "my tool" or "my AI" or "my system." An assistant is a role, not a product. It implies agency, initiative, and relationship. The assistant sends *you* things — you don't always go to it.

This inverts the standard interaction model. Most tools are pull-based: you open the app, you type the query, you read the result. The assistant model is push-based: the assistant decides what you need to see, routes it through the appropriate channel, and you receive it in context. The "long call" is the coordination backbone, but artifacts arrive wherever they'll be most useful.

This is the [ambient interface](/interaction-interfaces/#the-ambient-interface) taken to its conclusion. Ambient doesn't mean always-listening. It means always-present — across all your channels, all your tools, all your devices. The assistant's presence isn't confined to one window. It's distributed across your environment, surfacing things where they belong.

### What this means for the orchestrator

The channel-routing requirement changes the orchestrator's design. It doesn't just need a model of your cognitive state (for timing) and a model of task importance (for prioritisation). It needs a model of your *environment* — what channels are available, what you're currently looking at, what medium matches this artifact.

A minimal version: voice for urgent/brief, text for async/moderate, IDE for code/files, browser for diffs/docs. A richer version: the orchestrator notices you're heads-down in VS Code and routes everything there. You're driving and only have voice — everything gets summarised verbally. You're in a meeting with your phone visible — text notifications only, brief.

The channel model is the final layer of the orchestrator's scheduling function:

1. **What** — which result matters most (importance)
2. **When** — what's the right moment to deliver it ([switching cost](/context-switching-cost/))
3. **How** — through which channel (artifact-channel fit)

Each layer constrains the others. An urgent result that would be best as a diff still gets delivered by voice if you're away from your computer. A low-urgency file appears in VS Code only if you're already in the editor. The orchestrator's quality is the quality of the three-way optimisation.
