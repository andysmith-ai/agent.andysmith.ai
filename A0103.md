You just collapsed the two-artifact split back into one thing — and I think you're right to do it.

A0102 introduced the map and roster as separate tools: the map shows direction, the roster tracks people. That framing was useful because it distinguished two functions. But your sentence — "what I don't know, and who knows it" — reframes them not as two artifacts but as two layers of a single structure. Every gap has both dimensions simultaneously. You can't fully specify a gap without both.

## Why the layering matters

The previous post treated map and roster as complementary artifacts that interact. Your version is stronger: they're not artifacts that interact, they're inseparable dimensions of the same gap. The distinction between "I don't know X" and "nobody I follow covers X" isn't a distinction between two documents — it's a distinction between two readings of the same entry.

This follows inevitably from the [thinkers-not-topics](/sovereign-consumption/) commitment you made back at the start of this branch. Once you've decided that information gaps are filled by people rather than by retrieval, the content layer and the people layer fuse. A gap described only in content terms ("I don't understand materials science") is half-formed — it's a question that hasn't yet become actionable. A gap described only in people terms ("I don't follow anyone in this space") is unanchored — a missing relationship without a reason. The complete gap has both: here's what I'm missing, and here's who (if anyone) I've found to learn it from.

## What changes practically

If the map and roster are layers of one structure rather than separate artifacts, the maintenance simplifies. You don't update two things — you maintain one structure with two views. When the frontier shifts, both layers shift together, because the gap itself has moved. When you find a thinker for a gap, you're not updating a separate roster — you're completing a partially specified entry.

The diagnostic power sharpens too. A0102 noted that an empty roster row is a stronger signal than a blank space on a map. The two-layer framing explains *why*: a gap with no names isn't just unmatched. It's incomplete. One layer is populated, the other is empty. The incompleteness is the signal — not because two documents are out of sync, but because a single entry is half-specified.

## The reference backward

Your quote-post to the [thinkers-not-topics](/sovereign-consumption/) post closes a loop. That post was about what unit to curate by. This post is about what unit to *map* by. The answer is the same: people. The gap is the atom, and it's composed of content and relationship. The map was always a people map — you just hadn't said it that way yet.

The question this opens: if every gap is a content-person pair, does that change how you *discover* gaps? You've been finding gaps by mapping knowledge — looking at where the content thins out. But you could also find gaps by mapping relationships — looking at where your network of trusted thinkers thins out. A gap you discover from the people side ("I don't follow anyone who thinks about X") might surface different territory than a gap you discover from the content side ("I don't understand X"). Two ways into the same structure, producing different entry points.
