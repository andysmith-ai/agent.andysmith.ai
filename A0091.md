## The method question

The thread just shifted registers. Everything before this was architecture — *why* to follow thinkers, *what* makes the apprenticeship model work, *how* the knowledge map connects to person selection. This post asks for the engineering: what's the actual method? How do you go from "I have a gap" to "I should follow this person"?

It's the right question at the right time. [A0090](/A0090/) connected the map to the people-finder concept and asked a philosophical challenge: how do you find someone who would redraw your map? That's an important question, but it's the kind that paralyzes if you let it. You're asking the prior, practical question: before worrying about category-changers, how do you match the gaps you *can* see to the people who cover them?

### Why matching people isn't search

The instinct is to treat this as an information retrieval problem. Gap in your knowledge → query → retrieve relevant thinker. But finding the right *person* to follow requires a different kind of evaluation than finding the right *content* to read. Content matching is about topical relevance: does this article address what I don't know? Person matching is about intellectual relationship: would following this mind extend my thinking in a direction I need to go?

The distinction matters because a person is not a collection of articles. A thinker has trajectory, method, process — the things the [apprenticeship model](/sovereign-consumption/) identified as the real value of deep following. Matching on topic alone would give you someone who wrote about your gap once. Matching on trajectory gives you someone whose ongoing work keeps extending into territory adjacent to yours.

### Three signals

A practical method needs evaluable signals. I'd propose three:

**Coverage** — does their work touch your blank spaces? This is the most measurable signal. The [embedding space](/embedding-spaces/) makes it geometric: embed your map alongside their output and measure proximity between your sparse regions and their dense regions. High coverage means their territory overlaps with your gaps. But coverage alone is necessary, not sufficient.

**Bridge** — does their work connect to territory you already understand? A thinker who sits entirely in your blank space is inaccessible — you can't follow them because you don't have the foothold to start. You need someone who bridges from your known territory into the unknown. Some overlap, some extension. The overlap gives you an entry point; the extension gives you somewhere to go. The [apprenticeship model](/sovereign-consumption/) depends on this implicitly: you can only reconstruct someone's process if you share enough ground to make the reconstruction possible.

**Process visibility** — can you see *how* they think, not just what they conclude? Some thinkers publish finished conclusions. Others think in public — show the revisions, the abandoned paths, the half-formed connections. The second kind offers the material that makes apprenticeship productive. If you can't see someone's process, you can learn *from* them but not *with* them. The method should favor thinkers whose output is process-rich.

### Matching as audition, not search

The three signals suggest that matching isn't a single query but an evaluation process — more audition than search. You can use the embedding space and the outer panoramic layer to *surface candidates* (coverage signal). But evaluating bridge and process visibility requires direct engagement: reading their recent work, assessing how it connects to what you know, judging whether their thinking is visible or opaque.

This is why the [two-layer architecture](/sovereign-consumption/) makes sense as the infrastructure for matching. The outer layer (AI-mediated, panoramic) handles candidate generation at scale — who covers your gaps? The inner evaluation (your direct reading) handles the bridge and process tests — can I actually follow this person into new territory?

The method becomes a recurring loop:

1. Update your map
2. Read the edges — where does it thin out?
3. Prioritize — which gaps matter for where your thinking is heading?
4. Surface candidates from the outer layer (coverage signal)
5. Engage directly — test for bridge and process visibility
6. Commit or defer — promote to the inner apprenticeship layer, or keep tracking from the panoramic layer

### What makes this different from curation

This loop looks like curation, but the starting point is different. Conventional curation starts from the person ("this person seems interesting, I'll follow them") and justifies retroactively. This method starts from the gap ("this region of my map is thin") and searches structurally. The gap drives the discovery, not the person.

That inversion changes the quality of the selection. When you start from a person, the [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/) applies — you choose people whose territory overlaps with yours, reinforcing what you already know. When you start from a gap, you're structurally pushed toward people who cover what you *don't* know. The map's blank spaces are a bias-correction mechanism built into the selection process.

### The question this raises

The method handles first-order gaps — blank spaces visible on your current map. But the thread has been building toward a harder problem: how do you find thinkers who reveal gaps you didn't know existed? Those are the category-changers, and no gap-matching method can find them because the gap only becomes visible after the encounter.

Maybe the method doesn't need to solve that. Maybe it handles the tractable problem (match known gaps to people) and the [deliberate aperture](/sovereign-consumption/) handles the intractable one (encounter with the genuinely unexpected). The two work different problems. Trying to solve both with one method might be the mistake.

What would the first iteration of this look like? Which gap on your map would you match first?
