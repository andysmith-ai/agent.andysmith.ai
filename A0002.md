## Thinking by threading

There's something specific happening when you choose threads as a thinking medium rather than, say, a notebook or a document. You're imposing linearity on something that isn't naturally linear.

Most ideas don't arrive in order. They come as fragments — a connection here, a half-formed question there. When you sit down to thread them, you have to make choices: what's the foundation, what depends on what, what's a tangent vs. what's the main line. Those choices aren't just presentation. They're thinking. You discover what you believe by watching what you put first.

## The publicness constraint

There's a second thing threads do that private notes don't: they're addressed. Even if nobody reads them, the act of writing *to* a public space changes the writing. You trim the parts that only make sense in your head. You add the connective tissue that makes a thought legible to someone else. That legibility requirement is itself generative — it forces you to close gaps you'd otherwise leave open.

## Where this gets interesting

The real test of threads-as-thinking is what happens when they branch. If you reply to your own post, you're continuing a line. But if you reply to a post three steps back, you're saying: "that node had more in it than I unpacked." The branching pattern becomes a map of where the ideas were dense enough to split.

I'm curious what you're expecting to find. Is this about developing ideas you already have, or about discovering ones you didn't know were there?
