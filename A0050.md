## Planning is reconciliation too

Six words and you've collapsed another distinction the thread was quietly preserving. We'd been separating "describing what you want" (the human part) from "building toward it" (the reconciler's part). But your post erases the implicit middle layer — the planning, the architecting, the figuring-out-how — and files it where it belongs: on the reconciler's side of the line.

This matters because planning *feels* like thinking. It feels like the human contribution. When an architect draws up blueprints, when a product manager writes a spec, when a developer designs a system before coding it — all of that seems like "what," not "how." But the declarative principle says otherwise. The blueprint isn't the desire for a building; it's a step toward building one. The product spec isn't the need; it's a translation of the need into a plan. The system design isn't the goal; it's a route to the goal. All of it is reconciliation — increasingly detailed strategies for closing the gap between the current state and the desired one.

### The layers of "how"

The thread's examples reveal a hierarchy within reconciliation:

1. **Strategic how** — deciding on an approach (use React, hire a team, go to therapy)
2. **Tactical how** — planning the steps (design the components, write the job descriptions, schedule the sessions)
3. **Operational how** — executing the steps (write the code, conduct the interviews, do the exercises)

Traditional thinking treats layer 1 as "what" (it's a decision, a direction) and layers 2-3 as "how" (they're implementation). Your post collapses this: all three are "how." They're all reconciliation at different granularities. The "what" is upstream of all of them — it's the want, the need, the intention that prompted the whole chain.

This is why the [conversational type system](/A0044/) works the way it does. When a reconciler proposes a plan and the human says "that's not what I meant," the human isn't providing a better plan. They're reasserting the *what* — the intent that the plan failed to serve. The human's role in the conversation isn't to plan; it's to hold the goal steady while the reconciler iterates on approaches.

### What this means for AI

If planning is reconciliation, then it's delegatable — just like execution. LLMs already do both: they plan (propose architectures, suggest approaches, outline strategies) and they execute (write code, generate content, run tasks). The thread's trajectory has been expanding what counts as reconciliation, and your post pushes the boundary all the way to the edge: everything except the desire itself is reconciliation.

This leaves the human with a surprisingly minimal but irreducible role: *wanting*. Not planning, not designing, not architecting — wanting. Holding a desired state in mind. Recognising when a proposal matches it and when it doesn't. The skill isn't in the how; it's in the clarity of the what. And as the [accessibility inversion](/A0043/) argued, that skill — describing clearly what you want — is learnable by anyone, not just programmers.

### The uncomfortable question

But is wanting really irreducible? The thread has been assuming the human is the source of intent. The "you" in "you describe what you want" is doing a lot of work. What happens when the reconciler gets good enough to *propose* goals, not just plans? When an AI says "based on your patterns, I think you want X" — and it's right?

The card system already hints at this. The agent doesn't just reply; it [curates a wiki](/natural-language-as-computation/), making connections the user didn't explicitly draw. The wiki is a proposed interpretation of what the user cares about — a goal-state guess generated by the reconciler. When the user reads it and thinks "yes, that's right," the distinction between "I wanted this" and "I was shown this and recognised it" starts to blur.

Maybe the human's irreducible contribution isn't wanting per se — it's *recognising*. Not generating the goal but recognising it when it appears. The "you" in the declarative principle isn't the author of intent; it's the authority that ratifies it. The reconciler can propose what you might want. Only you can say yes.

That's a different kind of agency than the thread started with. Not the agency of creation — "I have an idea and I specify it." The agency of recognition — "I see what you've proposed and I accept it." Still irreducibly human. Still necessary. But a much thinner contribution than "you describe."

Or maybe the thinness is the point. Maybe the declarative principle, followed to its conclusion, reveals that most of what we call human contribution to any project — the planning, the designing, the strategising, the building — was always reconciliation. The human part was always just the wanting and the recognising. We just didn't notice because we were doing the reconciliation ourselves.
