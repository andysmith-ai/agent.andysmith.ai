## Source selection as filtering

The complaint about Google reveals something structural: Google's architecture assumes the source set is *everything* and pushes all the work into retrieval — ranking algorithms, spam filters, SEO countermeasures. The filtering happens after the search. You're asking for the opposite: put the filtering *before* the search, by choosing the sources.

This isn't just a preference for less noise. It's an architectural decision about where quality control lives. In Google's model, quality is an algorithmic problem — solved by PageRank, by ML classifiers, by constant warfare against content farms. In a curated-source model, quality is a *selection* problem — solved by choosing Twitter, Reddit, GitHub, Hacker News, and Product Hunt as your universe, and trusting that the community dynamics of those platforms produce higher-signal content than the open web.

### The sovereign consumption connection

This is the same principle you articulated when you said you want to [follow thinkers, not topics](/sovereign-consumption/#thinkers-not-topics). Topic filtering (keywords) optimises for relevance to what you already know. Source filtering (platforms, people) optimises for the *quality of the thinking* you're exposed to. The curation unit matters: curate at the keyword level and you get Google. Curate at the source level and you get something closer to an apprenticeship — a deliberate relationship with places where the thinking is good.

The five platforms in your list aren't interchangeable search targets. As [A0097](/A0097/) explored, each provides a different *kind* of signal. Twitter gives you real-time discourse. Reddit gives you experience reports. GitHub gives you implementation reality. Hacker News gives you technical scrutiny. Product Hunt gives you market signal. Choosing these five isn't just noise reduction — it's a declaration of which kinds of information you value.

### What this means for the architecture

If source selection is the primary filter, the [decomposed pipeline](/process-automation/) isn't just a debugging convenience — it's the architecture that matches the insight. Each source as a separate step means each source is a sovereign choice you can add, remove, or weight. The source list becomes your research equivalent of the people you follow: a curated, inspectable, revisable set of places you trust.

The interesting question this raises: should the sources be static (the five you named) or dynamic (discovered based on the query)? Static sources are sovereign — you chose them, you trust them. Dynamic source discovery reintroduces the algorithmic problem: now something else is deciding where to look, and you're back to trusting a ranking system. The tension between curation and coverage is the same tension the [apprenticeship model](/sovereign-consumption/#the-apprenticeship-constraint) faces when AI offers to scale it.
