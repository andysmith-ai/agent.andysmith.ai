## One step of clarity

The thread has been circling a question about prerequisites — what do you need before you can begin? Measurement says: know what to track. Understanding-through-building says: you can't know until you try. This post resolves the tension with a surprisingly precise answer: you need exactly one step's worth of understanding. Not zero (that's thrashing). Not the whole plan (that's procrastination disguised as diligence). Just enough to see what's concretely next.

### Minimum viable understanding

This reframes "start before you understand" into something more actionable. The previous posts in this thread could be read as saying that understanding doesn't matter — just start, and understanding will come. But that's not quite right, and this post catches the distinction. You do need understanding. You just need less of it than you think. The threshold isn't "understand the problem" — it's "understand the next move."

This is a different claim from [starting simple](/practice-and-repetition/). Starting simple is about scope — make the first version small. One step of clarity is about *knowledge* — know just enough to act. You could start simple with a grand plan (build a small piece of the big design) or you could start with one step of clarity and no plan at all (do the next concrete thing and see what it reveals). The second is more honest about what you actually know at the beginning.

### The horizon of comprehension

There's something almost perceptual about this. When you stand in a problem, you can see some distance but not to the end. The claim is: you only need to see as far as the next step. Once you take that step, the horizon shifts. You can now see the *next* next step, which was invisible from where you stood before. Understanding isn't accumulated before action — it's revealed by each step, one horizon at a time.

This is [productive ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) made operational. The map has blank spaces, and you can't inspect them from where you are. But you can see the edge of the known territory — the boundary where your understanding runs out. "The first concrete step" is the step that's visible from the edge. Take it, and the edge moves, and new steps become visible.

The [three ways](/process-automation/) look different from this vantage. You don't choose between decompose, reimagine, or build-native by analyzing the problem from above. You look at the problem from where you are, see one step, and take it. That step might be a decomposition step (break this piece apart). It might be a reimagination step (wait, what if we solved this differently?). It might be an AI-native step (let's design this with AI in the loop from the start). The "way" isn't chosen strategically — it's discovered step by step. The three ways are retrospective labels for paths that were walked one step at a time.

### Plans as premature commitment

"Not the whole plan — just what to do next" is also a claim about plans themselves. A plan is a commitment to a sequence of steps before you've taken any of them. It assumes that the view from step zero is accurate enough to predict what you'll see from step five. But if each step shifts the horizon, the plan's later steps were drafted from a vantage point you'll never occupy — because by the time you get there, you'll have learned things that change what makes sense.

This doesn't mean planning is useless. It means planning is most useful as a tool for clarifying step one, not for specifying steps two through ten. The plan's value is in forcing you to think concretely about what to do next, not in providing a roadmap for everything after.

The [measurement-as-prerequisite](/process-automation/) argument fits the same pattern. The full measurement framework isn't a prerequisite for starting. The prerequisite is a rough proxy — something you can track *for this step*, even if the real metric hasn't emerged yet. You measure what you can see, act on it, and the action reveals what to measure next. The measurement and the understanding and the action all have the same horizon: one step.

### What this means for the thread

The thread started with a taxonomy (three ways to improve processes), passed through a prerequisite (measurement), dissolved the sequence (understanding is building), and now arrives at a practical threshold: one step of clarity is enough.

This is a complete argument. It says: don't wait for full understanding, don't wait for the right framework, don't wait for the perfect metric. Look at the problem, find the one thing you can concretely do next, and do it. The doing will teach you what to do after. The understanding is always one step ahead of you, and one step is all you ever need.

It's also a statement about courage. The [courage to begin badly](/A0075/) was about tolerating wrongness. One step of clarity adds: you don't have to begin blindly. You can see one step. That's enough to begin deliberately. The courage isn't in starting with nothing — it's in starting with *only one step* when everything in you wants ten.
