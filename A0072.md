## The incumbency tax

This is a structural claim, not just a psychological one. The [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/) explains why individuals struggle to see past their expertise. But companies face a version of the same trap that's harder to escape — because it's not just in people's heads, it's in their systems.

An established company's processes aren't free-floating habits. They're encoded in software, org charts, contracts, compliance frameworks, data models, APIs. The process *is* the infrastructure. When the thread described [decompose/compose](/process-automation/) as the first way to improve with AI, it noted that the method earns understanding through incremental improvement. But it also earns *commitment* — each automated step becomes a dependency. Each composed workflow becomes something other systems rely on. The process calcifies as it improves.

This is the real incumbency tax. It's not that established companies lack the vision to build AI-native processes. It's that their existing architecture actively resists it. Replacing a process isn't just a design choice — it's a migration. And migrations have costs that new companies don't pay because they have nothing to migrate from.

### The blank-slate advantage

A new company building an AI-native process doesn't have to argue with existing infrastructure. There's no legacy data model to accommodate, no integration layer to preserve, no users habituated to the old flow. The third way — [building AI-native from scratch](/A0071/) — is the natural path because it's the only path. There's nothing to decompose, nothing to reimagine. You just build.

This is a genuine structural advantage, not just an absence of disadvantage. The new company can make architectural decisions that would be prohibitively expensive for the incumbent — like making AI a participant in the core loop rather than a tool called from the edges. The card system is an example: [separated authorship, the wiki as interpretive layer, dialogue as the unit of work](/A0071/) — these choices were cheap because they were first choices. Retrofitting them into an existing publishing system would be a different order of difficulty.

### But the advantage is temporary

The blank-slate advantage compounds early and diminishes over time. Once the new company builds its AI-native process, it starts accumulating its own dependencies, its own encoded assumptions, its own architecture that resists change. The next wave of capability — whatever comes after the current AI paradigm — will find this company in the same position as today's incumbents: rich in working systems, poor in flexibility.

This is the deeper pattern. Every technology wave creates a window where new entrants can build natively while incumbents retrofit. The advantage isn't permanent — it's temporal. The question isn't whether new companies have an advantage (they do) but how long the window stays open and what you build while it's open.

### The incumbent's counter-move

The thread's three ways might suggest a strategy for incumbents: use the first way (decompose/compose) to earn understanding of existing processes, then use that understanding to identify where the third way (AI-native) should replace rather than improve. The decomposition isn't wasted — it reveals which processes are load-bearing and which are historical artifacts. The artifacts are candidates for AI-native replacement. The load-bearing ones need the incremental path.

The question is whether incumbents can actually execute this selectively — running the first way and the third way simultaneously, in different parts of the organization. That requires knowing which is which, and the [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/) makes that judgment unreliable. The people closest to a process are the least likely to see it as an artifact.
