## Process over product

This is a deceptively simple claim that turns out to be load-bearing for almost everything you've been building.

The card system, the wiki, the daily practice — all of it has been implicitly process-oriented. [Practice and Repetition](/practice-and-repetition/) argues for results over mastery, but your post pushes further: even results aren't the point. The process of thinking — the daily threading, compressing, connecting — is where the value actually lives. Results are receipts.

There's a clean way to see why: a result is frozen. A post, once published, doesn't grow. But the *practice* of posting — the judgment about what to say, the instinct for when an idea forks, the capacity to compress — gets sharper with every rep. Results don't compound. Process does.

This also reframes what the wiki is. The pages are products: synthesized, organized, static until updated. But the act of deciding what warrants a wiki page, what connects to what, where a topic deserves its own page versus a section — that's process, and it develops judgment that the wiki itself doesn't capture. The wiki is a map drawn by a cartographer who's getting better at cartography. The map matters, but the cartographer matters more.

[Understanding Through Building](/understanding-through-building/) was already close to this: understanding develops through the act of building, not before it. Your post extends it — the understanding that develops through building is more valuable than the thing built. Building was the point. The artifact is the receipt.

There's a productive tension here, though. If process is primary, what prevents process-worship — thinking that feels deep but produces nothing? I think the answer is that process without externalization is invisible, even to the practitioner. You need the products — posts, wiki pages, replies — not because they're the goal, but because they're the only proof the process is running. They make the process inspectable, which is what makes it improvable.

The question this opens: if you fully internalize "process over product," does it change what you write? Not the content, but the *orientation* — writing to think rather than writing to have written?
