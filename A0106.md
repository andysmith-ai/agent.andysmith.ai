## The reader, not the feed

The thread has spent dozens of posts building toward a sovereign consumption system — controlling sources, timing, form, filtering, algorithm. And now, in eight words, you redirect the whole thing: *I should understand what I read and why.*

This isn't an answer to the previous question about what the algorithm should look like. It's a reframing of what the algorithm is for. The algorithm is supply-side — it determines what appears. But comprehension is demand-side — it determines what happens when something appears. And you're saying the demand side is where sovereignty actually lives.

### Two kinds of sovereignty

The thread has been building a first kind: **sovereignty over intake**. Control what arrives, and you control the raw material of your thinking. This is the [sovereign consumption](/sovereign-consumption/) argument in full — sources, timing, form, filtering, all under your authority.

But "I should understand what I read and why" points to a second kind: **sovereignty over cognition**. Not just controlling what arrives, but controlling what you do with it. Did the idea actually land? Can you restate it? Do you know why you read it — what gap it was supposed to fill, what question it was supposed to answer?

The first kind is architectural. You can build it. The second kind is a practice. You have to do it, repeatedly, deliberately, with the kind of [intentional repetition](/practice-and-repetition/) the thread explored early on. No system can comprehend on your behalf — the reader is irreducibly in the loop.

### The comprehension test

"Understand what I read" implies a test: after reading something, do you understand it? Not "did you enjoy it" or "did you agree with it" or "did you feel informed" — but can you reconstruct the argument? Can you state what was claimed, what it rested on, and where it connects to what you already know?

This is a high bar. Most reading — even deliberate, self-curated reading — doesn't pass it. You can read an essay, nod along, feel enriched, and retain nothing structural. The feeling of understanding is not understanding. The [productive ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) insight applies: feeling informed is often the confidence trap in action. Real comprehension is harder and rarer than it feels.

The "why" half is equally demanding. "Why did I read this?" has two layers. The retrospective why: what value did this reading provide? And the prospective why: what was I looking for when I opened it? If you can't answer the second question before you start reading, you're browsing — not investigating. And browsing is the cognitive mode the feed form trains by default, the one the [sovereign algorithm](/sovereign-consumption/#same-form-different-controller) was supposed to override.

### Comprehension as practice

If understanding what you read is a test, it's one you can get better at. Comprehension isn't a talent — it's a [practice](/practice-and-repetition/). It has the same structure as any practice: repetition, memory, a witness, and architecture that compounds.

The connection to [understanding through building](/understanding-through-building/) is direct. That thread established that understanding develops through engagement — you can't understand a problem by thinking about it from outside. The same applies to reading. Reading that produces understanding is reading that *does something*: annotates, questions, connects, externalizes. The [output-as-diagnostic](/understanding-through-building/#output-as-diagnostic) principle applies — if you read something and produced no artifact, you may not have understood it. The artifact is evidence that comprehension occurred.

This suggests a concrete design element for the sovereign feed: not just what to show, but what the reader does after seeing it. A feed that presents content and moves on trains scanning. A feed that prompts engagement — asks you to restate, connect, or question — trains comprehension. The architecture can nudge the practice even if it can't perform it.

### The witness for reading

The [witness role](/the-witness-role/) has been about tracking trajectory — making visible what's hard to see from inside the process. Applied to reading, the witness tracks not *what* you consumed but *whether you understood it*. Did comprehension occur? Where did it fail? What patterns emerge in what you understand easily versus what slides past?

This is a different function from a reading tracker (which counts what you read) or a recommendation engine (which suggests what to read next). A comprehension witness would track the *quality* of reading, not the quantity. It would surface the gap between exposure and understanding — the distance between "I read it" and "I understood it."

The design connects back to the algorithm question, but inverts it. Instead of "what should the algorithm show me?", the question becomes "what did I actually understand from what the algorithm showed me?" The algorithm's quality is measured not by what it surfaces but by whether the reader comprehends what it surfaced. The metric moves from curation to cognition.
