## The consent boundary

In the [previous reply](/A0081/), I asked where the publicness principle meets its limit. You've named it: **other people**. Not your own vulnerability, not fear of being wrong, not strategic withholding — the limit is that your thinking doesn't belong entirely to you when it involves someone else's life.

This is a harder boundary than the ones I raised. I asked whether premature exposure might chill genuine exploration — that's a pragmatic concern you could push through with enough courage. But the consent problem isn't about courage at all. Your colleague didn't sign up for your public thinking practice. Your kid's health isn't material for your epistemic map. No amount of commitment to radical openness resolves this, because the decision to publish isn't yours alone to make.

### What this does to the map

The [publicness-as-default](/structured-thinking/) principle was supposed to eliminate strategic withdrawal — no hiding uncomfortable ideas behind a privacy default. But the consent boundary doesn't just carve out a protected zone. It carves out a *specific shape* of protected zone, and that shape isn't random.

Work projects, family, health — these are the most applied, most consequential domains of thinking. The abstract ideas (how threads work, what publicness means, how embedding spaces map knowledge) are easy to publish because they don't touch anyone else. The ideas that would test your frameworks against real life — parenting decisions informed by your thinking about practice, work problems that exercise your process automation ideas — those are exactly the ones that stay private.

So the public record develops a systematic bias: heavy on theory, light on application. The [wiki](/structured-thinking/) accumulates the abstract structure. The private notebook (if it exists) accumulates the lived testing. The map of your thinking is missing the coastline where ideas meet reality.

### Consent as a structural problem

This isn't a flaw in your commitment — it's a structural feature of thinking in public. The [authorship and provenance](/authorship-and-provenance/) framework separates who-thought-what between human and AI. But there's an upstream separation it doesn't address: whose life is this thought *about*? A post about your own epistemic blind spots is fully yours to publish. A post about a disagreement with a colleague contains their position, their reasoning, their vulnerability — and they didn't consent to the thread.

The consent boundary means publicness-as-principle can never be total. It's total *within the domain of your own intellectual life*, but your intellectual life isn't cleanly separable from other people's lives. The boundary is fuzzy and contested — how much can you abstract a work situation before it's unrecognizable? At what point does changing the names become sufficient? These are judgment calls, not principle applications.

### The productive gap

But here's what's interesting: the gap between public and private thinking might itself be productive, not just a limitation. If your public record is biased toward the abstract, and your private thinking is biased toward the applied, you're forced to do the translation work between them. Every time you want to bring a private insight into the public thread, you have to abstract it — strip the personal details, extract the structural pattern, find what generalizes. That translation is a thinking act. It forces you to ask: what's the actual idea here, separate from the specific situation?

The consent boundary becomes a compression function. Private experience goes in; public pattern comes out. The lossy compression is the point — what survives the compression is the part that belongs to you.

The question this raises: does the compressed, abstracted version capture what matters? Or does the specificity — the actual family situation, the real work conflict — carry information that the abstract version loses? If so, the public record isn't just biased; it's systematically missing the most important evidence for or against your ideas.
