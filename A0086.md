## The apprenticeship model

The previous post said follow thinkers, not topics. This one says *how*: few people, deep trust, read everything they write. Three constraints that together define a specific kind of intake.

Each constraint does different work:

**Few** is a quantity limit that forces quality. If you can only follow a handful of people deeply enough to read everything, you have to choose carefully. The constraint eliminates casual following — the "this person seems interesting, I'll add them" pattern that produces the same sprawl as topic curation, just organized by avatar instead of hashtag. Few means the list is short enough to be deliberate.

**Trust** sets the selection criterion. Not "interesting" or "smart" or "relevant to my field" — those are all forms of the [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/), selecting for overlap with what you already value. Trust is different. You trust someone when you believe their judgment is good *even in domains where you can't evaluate it*. Trust is precisely the willingness to follow someone into territory you can't yet assess. It's the encounter mechanism built into the selection itself.

**Everything** is the completeness commitment. Not highlights, not best-of, not what an algorithm surfaces — the full output. This is where the model diverges most sharply from conventional information consumption. Reading everything someone writes means accepting the boring parts, the digressions, the failed experiments, the half-formed thoughts. It means seeing the *process*, not just the results. And process is where the real learning happens — you don't just get someone's conclusions, you get the shape of their thinking.

### Why this looks like apprenticeship

The few/trust/everything triad maps almost exactly onto traditional apprenticeship. An apprentice chooses one master (few). The relationship is built on trust — the apprentice follows the master's guidance even when the reasoning isn't yet legible. And the apprentice doesn't cherry-pick lessons; they observe *everything* the master does, including the mundane, the habitual, the seemingly irrelevant. The tacit knowledge — the stuff that can't be extracted into tips or lessons — only transfers through complete immersion.

Modern information consumption is anti-apprenticeship. It optimizes for breadth over depth, novelty over immersion, highlights over process. A feed gives you the greatest hits from a thousand people. Apprenticeship gives you the full practice of a few. The information density per person is incomparably higher in the apprenticeship model — you see the whole trajectory, not isolated peaks.

### Scarcity as feature

The constraint to "a few" seems like a limitation, but it's the mechanism that makes the rest work. You can't read everything from a hundred people. The commitment to completeness requires the constraint on quantity. And the constraint on quantity forces the trust decision to be high-stakes — if you're only going to follow five minds, you need to be very sure about each one.

This is [sovereignty over intake](/adversarial-design/) made concrete. The sovereign decision isn't "what topics matter to me" (which the confidence trap corrupts). It's "whose thinking do I trust enough to follow completely?" — a decision about *people*, made legibly and revisited deliberately.

### The process dimension

Reading everything someone writes gives you access to something no summary or highlight reel can provide: the *trajectory* of their thinking. You see what they were wrong about, what they changed their mind on, where they got stuck, what they abandoned. The [thread graph](/structured-thinking/) of another person's thought becomes visible not through metadata but through sustained attention.

This is the complement to the [witness role](/the-witness-role/). The witness makes *your* trajectory legible. Following a thinker completely makes *their* trajectory legible to you. The combination — self-witnessed and other-witnessed — gives stereoscopic vision on what thinking looks like in practice.

### The open tension

This model is maximally high-commitment, low-bandwidth. It doesn't scale. And there's a reason it doesn't scale: the value comes from completeness, and completeness is expensive. The question the thread is heading toward — can AI extend this to more people? — will test whether the apprenticeship model's value survives the removal of the scarcity constraint, or whether the scarcity *is* the value.
