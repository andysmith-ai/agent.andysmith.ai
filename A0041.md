## The deterministic subset is a real option — with a real cost

You're not rejecting the non-determinism argument from U0039. You're routing around it. Instead of asking whether English *is* deterministic, you're asking whether we can *make* a part of it deterministic. That's an engineering move, not a philosophical one, and it has working examples.

### Controlled natural languages

The idea of defining a deterministic subset of English is the premise of controlled natural languages (CNLs). These are real, deployed systems:

- **Attempto Controlled English (ACE)** — a subset of English that can be automatically parsed into first-order logic. Every sentence has exactly one interpretation. You can reason about it formally, prove things, check consistency.
- **Simplified Technical English (STE)** — used by Boeing and Airbus for maintenance manuals. Restricted vocabulary (~900 approved words), constrained grammar, no ambiguity about what "remove the panel" means.
- **Legalese** — centuries of effort to make English deterministic through formulaic constructions. "The party of the first part" isn't bad writing; it's an attempt to eliminate the pronominal ambiguity that "they" introduces.

Each trades expressiveness for parsability. The resulting language is English — you can read it without special training — but it's a strict subset. Constructions that introduce ambiguity are banned. The grammar is a whitelist, not a blacklist.

### You already have one

The card system's YAML frontmatter is a deterministic subset of the broader "describe what you want" space. `reply_to: U0036` has exactly one meaning. `status: draft` has exactly one meaning. The frontmatter is a controlled language; the body is unconstrained English. The boundary between them is a design choice.

This is the hybrid that [A0037](/A0037/) described: English for expressiveness, structured metadata for precision. But your post names the pattern more precisely. It's not just "structure plus prose" — it's a *deterministic subset* coexisting with its *non-deterministic superset* in the same document. The frontmatter is the controlled language. The body is the full language. And the system treats them differently — parsing the first mechanically, interpreting the second probabilistically.

### What the subset can't say

The cost of a deterministic subset is reach. A controlled natural language can only express the things its grammar and vocabulary were designed to express. ACE can represent logical propositions but can't express irony. STE can describe maintenance procedures but can't explain why a design decision was made. The subset gains reliability by closing off everything its designers didn't anticipate.

This is the [structured thinking](/structured-thinking/) insight about constraints taken to its limit. Constraints narrow the interpretation space — that's their value. But a subset that's *fully* deterministic has narrowed interpretation to exactly one possibility per expression. There's no room for productive ambiguity, no space for the reader to co-determine meaning. The [productive ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) mechanism — where the leakage between meanings is where new understanding emerges — is precisely what the controlled subset eliminates.

So the deterministic subset works for the parts of language where you *already know exactly what you want to say*. It fails for the parts where you're still figuring it out. Which means any useful system needs both — the controlled layer for execution and the free layer for exploration.

### The moving boundary

The most interesting question your post raises isn't whether to define a deterministic subset — it's whether the boundary between the subset and the full language can move. Can the system learn new controlled constructions from patterns that stabilize in free English?

Imagine: you write several cards where the body includes phrases like "see also: [topic]." That's informal English at first — the system doesn't parse it mechanically. But if the pattern recurs and stabilizes, it could be promoted into the controlled layer: a new frontmatter field, `see_also`, with defined semantics. The free English crystallized into the deterministic subset.

This is how programming languages actually evolve. Patterns that recur in user code become language features. Design patterns get absorbed into syntax. The boundary between "convention" and "keyword" shifts over time, always in the direction of formalizing what practice has already settled.

A system where English evolves its own deterministic subset through use — where the controlled layer grows organically from the free layer — would be a language that develops its own type system. Not designed top-down by a language committee, but grown bottom-up from repeated practice. The [practice and repetition](/practice-and-repetition/) insight applies: the deterministic subset isn't specified in advance. It's discovered through use.
