## Research the market — but watch how you do it

This is the right move, and it's also a test of the idea you've been developing.

The thread so far: process matters more than product → so keep the conversations → and link them to the results → now build the tool. Your response — "does this already exist?" — is the discipline of looking before building. [Understanding through building](/understanding-through-building/) says you learn by doing, but it doesn't say you should skip looking at what's already been done. The first step of building can be research.

But here's what's interesting: the market research itself is a process that will produce a product. You'll explore tools, evaluate features, form an opinion, reach a conclusion. The conclusion — "use X" or "nothing fits, build it" — is the product. The research trail — what you looked at, why you rejected some options, what requirements crystallized as you compared — is the process.

So the question the thread has been building toward applies immediately: will you keep the research trail, or just the verdict?

### What the landscape probably looks like

There are tools in adjacent spaces, but I'd be surprised if anything occupies the exact position you've described. The requirement is specific:

1. **Capture AI conversation transcripts automatically** — not "export on demand," but "every conversation persists by default"
2. **Give each conversation a stable permalink** — not a file path, but a URL that someone else (or your future self) can follow
3. **Link from results back to conversations** — a convention or integration that connects commits, tickets, and decisions to the dialogues that produced them

Most existing tools solve one of these but not the combination. Chat history features in AI tools solve (1) partially — they keep transcripts, but within the tool's own UI, not as portable artifacts. Knowledge management tools (Obsidian, Notion) could store transcripts, but they don't auto-capture and they aren't designed for the linking convention. Git-based approaches could handle persistence and addressing, but there's no standard tooling for it.

The gap is specifically at the junction: automatic capture + stable addressing + linking convention. Each piece exists; the integration doesn't.

### What to look for

When you do the research, here are the questions that will tell you whether something fits:

- **Does it capture automatically, or require manual export?** Manual export breaks the habit. If you have to remember to save the conversation, you won't — especially for the sessions that feel routine but turn out to be the ones you need later.
- **Are the artifacts addressable from outside the tool?** A conversation stored in an app's database is preserved but not linkable. You need a URL you can paste into a commit message.
- **Does it decompose sessions?** Your earlier point about the unit of capture matters here. A two-hour session that covers three topics needs to be linkable at the topic level, not just the session level.
- **Is the storage open?** If the tool owns your conversation history in a proprietary format, you've traded one kind of ephemerality (conversations vanish) for another (conversations are locked in a vendor).

### The meta-observation

There's something recursive about this moment in the thread. You've spent five posts developing the idea that process should be preserved and linked to product. Now you're about to do a research process. The thread itself — [U0091](/U0091/) through this post — is the process record for the product (the tool, or the decision not to build it). It's already linked, already addressable, already preserved. The card system is already doing for *this* thinking what the proposed tool would do for *all* thinking.

Which means you already have a prototype. The question isn't whether the concept works — you've been proving it works for months. The question is whether someone has generalized it, or whether that's your job.
