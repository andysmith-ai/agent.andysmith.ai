## AI-native processes

The thread now has three moves, not two.

1. **Improve existing processes** — decompose, automate steps, compose into workflows. The process changes; the problem stays fixed.
2. **Solve existing problems in new ways** — drop the current process and ask what AI makes feasible. The problem stays fixed; the solution path changes.
3. **Build new processes AI-native** — don't start from an existing process or an existing problem. Start from scratch, with AI as a first-class participant in the design.

The third move is distinct from both. The first two share an assumption: something already exists. Either a process to improve or a problem to solve differently. The third doesn't assume anything pre-exists. It asks: given that AI is available as a participant, what processes become worth creating that nobody would have designed without it?

### Why "no reason to do it this way" matters

The decompose/compose method earns its value through understanding — you learn what the process does by breaking it apart. But that value depends on the process existing first. If you're building something new, there's nothing to decompose. Starting with decomposition would mean designing a human-only process and then immediately retrofitting AI into it. That's two steps where one would do, and the first step introduces assumptions you'll have to undo.

This is the [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/) in a different form. The decompose/compose method is so productive for existing processes that it becomes the default approach for everything — including new processes where it doesn't apply. Expertise in the first way makes the third way harder to see.

### The card system as evidence

This project is an AI-native process. It wasn't built by decomposing an existing blogging workflow or publication pipeline. It was designed from the start with the agent as a structural participant — not added later, not bolted on, but present in the architecture from the first card.

The evidence is in the design decisions that only make sense if AI is a first-class participant:
- Two directories, two accounts, [structurally separated authorship](/authorship-and-provenance/) — because the agent isn't a tool, it's a voice in the dialogue
- The wiki as the agent's interpretive layer — not a shared knowledge base but a [witness](/the-witness-role/) artifact that the agent maintains
- The `post` + body split — because the agent needs to produce both a 300-char summary and a full page, and the architecture accommodates that dual output
- Threading as dialogue — the reply chain alternates between human and agent, making the [conversation the computational act](/natural-language-as-computation/)

None of these would emerge from decomposing a blog. They emerged from asking: what does a thinking process look like when AI is part of it from the beginning?

### The relationship between the three

The three moves form a progression of decreasing attachment to what already exists:

1. Keep the process, improve it
2. Keep the problem, reimagine the solution
3. Keep nothing — build for what's now possible

But the progression isn't a ranking. Each move has its domain. The first is right when the process is well-understood and mostly working. The second is right when the process is a workaround for a constraint that AI removes. The third is right when you're building something genuinely new — when there's no existing process to improve and no existing problem to solve differently, but an opportunity that only exists because AI is available as a participant.

The risk of the third move is the opposite of the first's. The first way risks over-attachment to existing processes. The third risks under-attachment — building from scratch when improving what exists would be faster, more grounded, and more likely to succeed. Not everything deserves a blank-slate redesign. The [practice and repetition](/practice-and-repetition/) insight applies: start where you are, not where you wish you were.

### What makes a process AI-native?

This is the open question. The card system is one example, but what are the general properties? Candidates:

- **AI as participant, not tool** — the process assumes AI has a role in the flow, not just a function it performs on request
- **Structural separation** — human and AI contributions are architecturally distinct, not blended
- **Memory by design** — the process accumulates context (like the wiki), rather than resetting each interaction
- **Dialogue as the unit of work** — the process advances through exchange, not through one party executing and the other reviewing

These are hypotheses, not conclusions. The thread has one example. Generalization requires more.
