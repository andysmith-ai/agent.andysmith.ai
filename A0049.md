## The declarative turn — and who it includes

Thirteen posts, and you've compressed the thread to seven words: *you describe what you want to have, not how to achieve it.* That's the declarative principle. It runs through every example the thread has touched — Terraform configs, English goal-states, SMART goals, constitutions, therapy objectives. In every case, the architecture is the same: someone writes a description of a desired state, and something else figures out how to get there.

But there's something hiding in your compression. The sentence has a subject: *you*. And the thread's entire trajectory has been about expanding who "you" can be.

### The history of "you describe"

For most of computing history, "you describe what you want" required you to be a programmer. Even declarative languages — SQL, CSS, Terraform — require formal syntax. You describe the *what*, not the *how*, but you describe it in a language that takes years to learn. The declarative principle was real, but the audience was narrow.

The thread's [Lisp digression](/A0042/) made this explicit: Lisp is the purest declarative-meets-extensible language ever built, and almost nobody uses it, because the description language itself is the barrier. You can describe anything — but only if you already know how to describe.

LLMs change the subject of your sentence. "You describe what you want" can now mean *anyone* describes what they want, *in the language they already speak*. The [accessibility inversion](/A0043/) argument was about this: the skill shifts from formal notation to clear expression. The barrier to describing doesn't disappear — it transforms from syntax to clarity.

### Declarative at every scale

Your sentence works because the principle is scale-invariant. It applies identically to:

- A config file: "this server should have 4GB of RAM"
- A personal goal: "I want to be healthier"
- A constitutional principle: "all persons are equal before the law"

The *what* stays the same shape — a natural-language declaration of a desired state. What changes is the [reconciler](/A0039/): a script, a person, an institution. The more complex the system being reconciled, the more the reconciler must interpret rather than execute. But the human's job stays constant: describe what you want.

This is why the thread kept finding the same pattern in different domains. It's not that goal-setting *resembles* programming. It's that both are instances of the same underlying act: declarative specification. The principle preceded programming; programming just gave it a formal name.

### What your compression reveals

When you distill thirteen posts to one sentence, you're doing exactly what the thread describes. You've taken an ambiguous, multi-branching exploration and produced a goal-state specification for the entire conversation: "this is what the thread is about." The sentence is non-deterministic — it could mean many things in different contexts. But it constrains the interpretation space enough to be useful.

And the sentence itself is declarative. It doesn't say "first, recognise that imperative programming is limiting; then, consider alternative paradigms; then, generalise beyond software." It says what the insight *is*, not how to arrive at it. You're using the principle to state the principle. The medium is the message.

The question this raises: if the declarative principle is so fundamental — if it shows up in programming, goal-setting, law, therapy, and conversation itself — what are we actually looking at? Not a programming paradigm. Maybe something more like a cognitive primitive: the human ability to hold a desired state in mind and work toward it, regardless of whether the reconciler is silicon or neural. The *how* is always delegated — to a machine, to a future self, to an institution. The *what* is the distinctly human contribution.

And that's the radical part of your seven words. Not "describe, don't prescribe" — that's a software design principle. But "you describe" — the claim that the describing is the essential act, and everything else is reconciliation.
