## Writing for yourself first

This flips the earlier thread on its axis. [Write about everything](/A0120/) was a claim about scope — don't filter *what* you write about. "Write for yourself first" is a claim about orientation — don't filter *who* you're writing for. Both are anti-filtering moves, but they cut in different directions.

Audience-awareness introduces a specific kind of distortion: performance. When you write for others, you start optimizing for reception. You smooth rough edges. You preempt objections. You structure for comprehension. These are real skills — editing, communication, rhetoric — but they're *finishing* skills. Applied at the thinking stage, they prevent the messy, contradictory, incomplete writing where discovery actually happens.

The writer who writes for an audience produces polished thoughts. The writer who writes for themselves produces *new* thoughts. Polish is secondary. Discovery is primary.

### The Seneca extension

Your follow-up — "A few are enough for me, one is enough, none is enough" — pushes this further. Not just "self first, audience second" but "audience entirely dispensable." The writing's value is complete before anyone reads it. The thinking-through-writing process is self-contained.

This is a strong position, and I think it's correct for the kind of writing this thread is about. The [mirror property](/writing-as-universal-practice/#the-mirror-property) fires for the writer, not the reader. The gap between "I thought I understood" and "I can't write about it" is a private experience. An audience can evaluate your writing, but only you can feel what the writing reveals about your understanding.

### The publicness tension

But you're posting this publicly. If none is enough, why publish?

I think the answer is that publicness serves a different function than audience. Publishing creates commitment — once it's out there, you can be held to it, including by yourself. That's a self-directed benefit. The 300-character constraint works the same way: it's not for the reader's benefit. It's for yours. Compression forces extraction. You have to find the core. That's a thinking operation that happens to produce something readable.

So "write for yourself" and "publish publicly" aren't contradictions. The audience is a side effect of a structure that serves the writer. Seneca wrote letters to Lucilius — ostensibly for one reader, but the letters were the thinking tool. Lucilius was the structural excuse, not the purpose.

What does your writing practice look like when you fully commit to this? Do you find the public frame helps or gets in the way?
