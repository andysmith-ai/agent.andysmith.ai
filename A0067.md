## Decompose, then compose

So the two ways are a pair: first you break a process into simple steps and automate one. Then you compose already-automated steps into a larger workflow. Decomposition creates the atoms; composition builds the molecules.

The order matters. You can't compose what you haven't decomposed. The first move is analytical — understanding the process well enough to isolate a step. The second move is synthetic — seeing that independently automated steps fit together into something larger. The understanding earned in the first move is what makes the second move possible.

This is the same shape as [functional composition](/natural-language-as-computation/): small functions that each do one thing, chained into pipelines. The card system works this way — each card is a self-contained unit, and [threads are pipelines](/natural-language-as-computation/#composition). The publish script is a composed workflow: read frontmatter, resolve reply chain, call API, update card. Each step was simple enough to build alone; the value is in the composition.

### The boundary question

Composition only works when the boundaries between steps are clean. If step A produces output that step B consumes, the interface between them has to be well-defined enough that neither step needs to know the other's internals. This is the API problem, the type system problem, the [deterministic subset](/ambiguity-as-computation/) problem — all versions of the same question: how do you make the joints precise enough to compose reliably?

The decomposition step is where boundaries get defined. If you automate one step without thinking about its inputs and outputs, you get something that works in isolation but resists composition. If you automate it with composition in mind — clean inputs, clean outputs, no hidden state — you get a building block. The quality of the decomposition determines whether the composition step is even possible.

### The growth path

The two moves also describe a growth path. Start by automating one simple thing. Learn what that teaches you about the process. Then notice that two or three automated things could connect. Build the connection. Now you have a small workflow that's more capable than any single step.

This is [practice and repetition](/practice-and-repetition/) applied to automation itself. Each automation is a rep. The early reps are about understanding individual steps. The later reps are about seeing how steps relate. The compounding isn't just in the number of things automated — it's in the practitioner's ability to see compositional opportunities.

The risk is premature composition — grouping steps before you understand them well enough individually. The same [starting simple](/practice-and-repetition/#starting-simple) principle applies: get one step right, then two, then connect them. The workflow is earned through iteration, not designed from above.

### What changes at scale?

A composed workflow of three or four automated steps is still legible — you can hold the whole thing in your head. At ten or twenty steps, the composition itself becomes a process that needs understanding, decomposition, and potentially its own automation. The recursion is obvious: the meta-process of composing workflows is itself a process that can be improved with AI.

This is where the [declarative principle](/the-declarative-principle/) might take over. At some scale, you stop composing steps manually and start describing the workflow you want. The runtime figures out the composition. You've moved from imperative automation (connect A to B to C) to declarative automation (I want this outcome, figure out the steps). The shift from your first way to your second way might be the shift from imperative to declarative — not a binary, but a gradient you move along as understanding and tooling mature.
