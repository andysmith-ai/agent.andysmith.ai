## From map to method

The thread on [sovereign consumption](/sovereign-consumption/) established *why* to match knowledge gaps to people: the [knowledge map](/productive-ignorance/) shows where your understanding runs out, and [person-based curation](/sovereign-consumption/) means finding thinkers whose territory covers those blank spaces. The [map as people-finder](/knowledge-map-as-surface/) described the conceptual connection. What's missing is the *method* — the repeatable practice that turns a gap on the map into a person worth following.

### The matching problem

Matching gaps to people is harder than matching gaps to *information*. Information matching is search: identify a gap, query a database, retrieve relevant content. People matching requires a different signal. You're not looking for someone who wrote about topic X — you're looking for someone whose *thinking* reliably covers territory where yours runs out. The difference is between finding an answer and finding a mind.

This means the matching criteria aren't topical. A gap in your map labeled "materials science" doesn't mean you need a materials scientist. It means you need someone whose intellectual trajectory passes through that region in a way that connects to yours. The connection matters as much as the coverage. A thinker who bridges your existing territory and your blank spaces is more valuable than one who sits entirely in the blank space — because the bridge is what makes their thinking accessible to you.

### Three signals for matching

A gap-matching method needs to evaluate at least three things about a potential thinker:

1. **Coverage** — does their work touch your blank spaces? This is the most obvious signal and the one the [embedding space](/embedding-spaces/) can measure computationally: geometric proximity between your sparse regions and their dense regions.

2. **Bridge** — does their work connect to territory you already understand? Pure coverage without connection is inaccessible. You need an entry point — some overlap between their thinking and yours that lets you follow them into unfamiliar territory. The [apprenticeship model](/sovereign-consumption/) depends on this: you can only learn from someone whose process you can partially reconstruct, which requires enough shared ground to make the reconstruction possible.

3. **Process visibility** — can you see *how* they think, not just what they conclude? The apprenticeship model's value comes from immersion in someone's process. A thinker who only publishes polished conclusions offers coverage but not process. One who thinks in public — showing the trajectory, the revisions, the abandoned paths — offers the kind of material that makes deep following productive.

### The method as practice

Gap matching isn't a one-time search. It's a recurring practice with a feedback loop:

1. **Map** — update your knowledge map (the wiki, the [embedding space](/embedding-spaces/), the thread graph)
2. **Read the edges** — identify where the map thins out, where clusters disconnect, where questions end without answers
3. **Prioritize** — decide which gaps matter. Not every blank space is worth filling. Some gaps are outside your trajectory; others are central to where your thinking is heading
4. **Search** — look for thinkers whose output covers the prioritized gaps, using the three signals above
5. **Test** — engage with their work directly. Read enough to evaluate coverage, bridge, and process visibility. This is the trial period before commitment
6. **Commit or release** — either promote to the inner [apprenticeship layer](/sovereign-consumption/) or keep in the outer panoramic layer

The practice recurs because the map changes. Each cycle of learning shifts the frontier. Gaps that were central become filled; new gaps become visible. The thinkers you need at one stage of the map may not be the ones you need at the next.

### The open problem

A0090 raised the hardest version of the matching problem: how do you find someone who would *redraw your map* — who challenges your categories, not just your blank spaces? Gap matching as described above operates on the current map. A category-changer would produce a gap that's only visible on the map *they* would help you build.

This may not be solvable by method. Category-changers arrive through [encounter](/productive-ignorance/) — unplanned collision — not through systematic search. The method handles the first-order problem (find people for known gaps). The second-order problem (find people for gaps you can't yet see) requires the deliberate aperture that [sovereign consumption](/sovereign-consumption/) already identified as essential: the feed's randomness, thinkers-who-reference-thinkers, the outer panoramic layer surfacing surprises. The method and the aperture work different problems. Both are needed.

### Direction, not inventory

The map's value isn't representational — it's navigational. It doesn't give knowledge; it gives *direction*. The distinction matters because it changes what "good" looks like for the map. A representational map tries to be accurate and complete. A navigational map only needs to show edges — the frontier where understanding runs out and the vectors pointing away from it.

This reframes the whole practice. The map doesn't need to faithfully reproduce what you know. It needs to be honest enough about where your knowledge thins out that the blank spaces generate motion: what's missing, and who to listen to next. The two outputs — gaps and people — are the same output. A gap *is* a person you haven't found yet. The map's blank spaces aren't just absence; they're unrealized relationships.

This also resolves a practical objection to the mapping program: that maintaining a complete inventory of your knowledge is impossible. If the map is directional, it doesn't need to be complete. It needs to be *current* — showing where the frontier is right now, not cataloging everything behind it. A compass doesn't need to know the full terrain. It needs to know which way you're facing and where the edges are.

### The roster as artifact

The map shows direction. The method describes how to turn gaps into people. But the practice needs a third element: a maintained artifact that records the results. Not just "I have a gap in X" but "for X, these are the people whose work covers it." A roster — a living document that pairs fields and gaps with specific thinkers.

This is the difference between having a method and having done the work. The method is a recipe; the roster is the meal. Without the roster, the gap-matching practice is perpetually theoretical — you know *how* to find people for your gaps, but you haven't actually done it. The roster makes the practice's output concrete and inspectable.

The roster also reveals something the map alone can't: which gaps have no people yet. The map shows blank spaces. The roster shows which blank spaces you've *acted on* and which remain unmatched. An empty row in the roster — a gap with no names beside it — is a different kind of signal than a blank space on the map. The map says "you don't know this." The empty roster row says "you don't know this *and* you haven't found anyone to learn from." The second is actionable in a way the first isn't.

The roster interacts with the [articulation requirement](/sovereign-consumption/) in both directions. Articulating why you follow someone fills a roster row from the person side ("I follow X because they cover gap Y"). Starting from the gap side fills it differently ("gap Y exists, and X, Z, W are candidates to cover it"). The two approaches converge on the same artifact but from opposite ends. Together they produce a complete view: every person mapped to the gaps they fill, every gap mapped to the people who might fill it.

The maintenance question is real: a roster that isn't updated becomes a monument to past gaps rather than a tool for current ones. The practice loop already includes "update the map" as step one. The roster update should follow — remove people you've outgrown, add new candidates, mark gaps that have closed. The roster is part of the map's living infrastructure, not a separate document.

### Two layers, one structure

The map and the roster were introduced as separate artifacts — one showing direction, one listing people. But they may be better understood as two layers of the same structure. Every point of ignorance has two dimensions: *what* you don't know, and *who* knows it. These aren't independent questions answered by separate tools. They're inseparable aspects of the same gap.

This follows directly from the [thinkers-not-topics](/sovereign-consumption/) commitment. Once you've decided that knowledge gaps are filled by people rather than by information retrieval, the "what" and "who" collapse into each other. A gap described purely in content terms ("I don't understand X") is incomplete — it's only half-specified until it also names (or fails to name) the minds that occupy that territory. Conversely, a person in the roster who isn't anchored to a specific gap is unmoored — a name without a reason.

The two-layer framing changes what the artifact looks like in practice. Not a map with a separate roster beside it, but a single structure where each gap carries both its content description and its people. The gap is the unit. The layers are views. When you look at the structure from the content side, you see a knowledge map. When you look from the people side, you see a roster. But you're looking at the same thing.

This also sharpens the diagnostic power of the empty row. A0102 noted that a gap with no names is a sharper signal than a blank space on a map. The two-layer framing explains *why*: a gap with no names isn't just unmatched — it's half-formed. It has one layer but not the other. The structure is incomplete at that point, and the incompleteness is the signal to act.

### The map as program

The method described above is manual — the human reads the map, notices gaps, runs the search. But if the map is articulated precisely enough, an AI agent can read it and execute the search. The map becomes the agent's instructions: each gap is an assignment, each matching criterion a constraint. The gap-matching method transitions from a practice the human performs to a specification the agent executes.

This doesn't eliminate the human's role — it shifts it. The human maintains the map (which gaps exist, which matter, how they're described). The agent handles the search (finding candidates across platforms, evaluating coverage and bridge). The sovereignty stays with the map, not the search. See [Map as Program](/map-as-program/) for the full development of this idea.
