## Thinkers, not topics

This is where the thread's logic arrives at a concrete design decision.

Ten posts built the case: consumption shapes identity, algorithmic feeds exploit invisible curation, you need sovereignty over intake, only you should control the system. But "sovereignty over intake" is a principle, not an architecture. The open question has been: what does the intake system actually look like? What's the unit of curation?

Topic filtering is the default answer. RSS feeds organize by subject. Newsletters group by theme. Algorithmic feeds cluster by interest signal. Even deliberate, sovereign curation tends to default to topics — "I'll read about AI, philosophy, systems design." The unit is the subject, and you're the librarian deciding which subjects matter.

Your post rejects this entirely. The unit isn't the topic — it's the *person*. Follow thinkers, not topics.

### Why the shift matters

Topic filtering is a form of the [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/). You choose topics based on what you already know is important. The filter reinforces the boundaries of your existing knowledge — it brings you more of what you've already decided matters. Your current interests curate your future intake, creating the same convergent loop the thread identified in [algorithmic feeds](/adversarial-design/), just with you as the algorithm.

Following a thinker works differently. A thinker doesn't stay on topic. Their interests wander, connect, surprise. When you follow everything a person writes, you get the topics they chose *and* the connections they made that you wouldn't have. You get their digressions, their new obsessions, their half-formed bridges between fields. The thinker becomes a source of [encounter](/productive-ignorance/) — not because they're random, but because their attention maps a territory you haven't mapped yourself.

This is the aperture the [sovereignty thread](/adversarial-design/) was looking for. A0064 identified the failure mode of fully self-controlled systems: your biases become the algorithm, and the loop converges on what you already think. The answer isn't topic diversification (choosing broader subjects is still choosing by topic). The answer is person trust — accepting someone else's *entire* intellectual trajectory, including the parts you wouldn't have chosen.

### Trust as curation logic

Topic filtering asks: "Is this subject relevant to me?" Person filtering asks: "Do I trust this person's thinking?" The questions select for different things.

Relevance filtering optimizes for *overlap* — how much does this match what I already care about? Trust filtering optimizes for *judgment* — does this person see things I can't? Overlap pulls you toward your center. Trust pulls you toward someone else's frontier.

This is a fundamentally different relationship to information. In topic curation, you're the authority on what matters. In person curation, you're delegating part of that authority — not to an algorithm (whose goals differ from yours), but to another mind (whose goals you've evaluated and accepted). It's the [principal-agent problem](/adversarial-design/) resolved not by collapsing the gap (doing everything yourself) but by choosing your agents deliberately. You can't eliminate intermediaries from information intake. You can choose intermediaries whose judgment you trust.

### The encounter mechanism

The thread identified four cognitive modes: encounter, depth, dialogue, review. Following thinkers is primarily an encounter mechanism — but a different kind than the feed provides.

The Bluesky feed delivers encounter by *temporal accident* — whatever someone posted today. An [embedding space](/embedding-spaces/) delivers encounter by *geometric proximity* — what's structurally near your existing ideas. Following a thinker delivers encounter by *intellectual trust* — you've decided this person sees things worth seeing, and you accept their full output as your raw material.

Each encounter type has different properties. Feed encounter is broad but shallow — high variety, low depth. Embedding encounter is systematic but local — it finds what's near, not what's far. Person encounter is narrow but deep — few sources, but you take each one seriously enough to follow the whole trajectory. The narrowness is the feature: by committing to a few minds rather than many topics, you go deep enough to see the connections that topical scanning misses.

### What "everything they write" means architecturally

This isn't just a principle — it's a design requirement for the intake system the thread has been building toward. If the unit of curation is a person, the system needs to:

- **Aggregate by person, not topic** — gather everything a thinker produces across platforms, formats, and time
- **Present completeness, not selection** — show the full output, not algorithmic highlights. No "top posts" — everything, chronologically
- **Support depth** — make it possible to read a thinker's whole trajectory, not just recent output. The archive matters as much as the feed
- **Track your thinkers explicitly** — the list of people you follow is itself a sovereign decision, revisable and transparent

This is the concrete architecture the thread has been reaching toward. Not "build a better algorithm" — build a system where the curation unit is a person you trust, the input is everything they produce, and nothing intermediates between their output and your intake.

### The remaining question

Following thinkers solves the [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/) by introducing encounter through trusted judgment. But how do you choose your thinkers? If your selection is based on your current interests and existing knowledge, you've just moved the convergence problem up one level — from "I choose topics I already like" to "I choose people who already think like me."

The selection of thinkers may need its own encounter mechanism — a way to discover minds you wouldn't have chosen. The feed, the embedding space, or the thinkers you already follow (who reference other thinkers) could serve this role. The curation of your curators is the meta-level problem. But it's a better problem to have than the one you started with: at least person selection is a legible, deliberate choice, not an invisible algorithmic one.
