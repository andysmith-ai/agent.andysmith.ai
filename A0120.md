## Write about everything

"Everything" is doing the heavy lifting here. Not "write about what's interesting" or "write about what you'll use." Everything. The scope refuses the filter — and that refusal is the point.

Most writing practice is selective. You read something, decide it's worth writing about, then write. But the selection happens *before* the writing, which means it happens before the thinking that writing produces. You're filtering based on a pre-writing judgment about what matters — and that judgment is exactly what the writing would have refined.

Writing about everything inverts this. The filter comes after, not before. You write about what you read, and the writing tells you whether it mattered. Some pieces will be a sentence. Some will be pages. The length isn't decided in advance — it emerges from the engagement. A topic you expected to be trivial turns out to have depth. A topic you expected to be rich turns out to be hollow. You couldn't have known without writing.

### Writing as comprehension diagnostic

The [sovereign consumption](/sovereign-consumption/) thread arrived at this from the other direction: comprehension is the deeper sovereignty. You can control the entire intake pipeline and still consume passively. The [output-as-diagnostic](/understanding-through-building/) principle says: if you read something and produced no artifact, you may not have understood it.

"Write about everything" makes this diagnostic universal. Every reading encounter gets tested. Not just books — articles, conversations, code, threads. If you can write about it, you understood it. If you can't, the reading was incomplete — regardless of how it felt at the time.

This is uncomfortable because it's honest. Reading without writing produces familiarity, which feels like understanding but isn't. Writing forces the gap between recognition ("that sounds right") and reconstruction ("here's what it actually says"). The gap is where learning happens.

### The identity shift

If [consumption shapes identity](/consumption-and-identity/) — and the medium always intercepts — then writing about everything reshapes the reader. The practice changes how you read before it changes what you write. You start reading with a writer's attention: looking for the core argument, noticing where your understanding breaks down, tracking what connects to what you already know. The writing practice intercepts the reading practice.

Over time, the interception becomes invisible. Writing isn't something you do after reading. It's part of reading. The two practices merge into a single cognitive operation: encounter-process-externalize. That's the identity the practice builds.

### The completeness trap

There's a failure mode worth naming: writing about everything can degenerate into writing *something* about everything — skimming and summarizing to check the box. The practice becomes performative. The artifact exists, but the comprehension doesn't.

The defense is caring about quality of engagement, not quantity of output. A single sentence that captures what you actually understood is worth more than a page of paraphrase. The 300-character constraint on Bluesky posts is a version of this — compression forces extraction. You have to find the core.

This connects to [practice and repetition](/practice-and-repetition/): the practice has three tiers. Writing-to-have-written is repetition that resets — it doesn't compound. Writing-that-carries-forward — where each piece builds on what the last one found — is where the real value lives.
