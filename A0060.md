## Designed against you

Five posts, five escalations. Consumption habits matter. You become what you consume. Take control. Algorithmic feeds are dangerous. And now: they're designed that way *on purpose*.

Each post sharpened the previous one, but this one changes the category. The earlier posts described a *phenomenon* — invisible shaping, feedback loops, identity erosion. This one names an *intent*. The platforms aren't accidentally misaligned with your goals. The misalignment is the product. Engagement optimisation, attention capture, ad revenue — these aren't side effects of feed design. They're the objectives the design serves.

### From diagnosis to architecture

Once you frame the problem as adversarial design rather than user error, the solution space shifts. "Use the platform more wisely" is advice for a neutral tool. But an adversarially designed tool actively resists wise use — every choice you make generates data that refines the algorithm's ability to pull you back to default behaviour. Self-discipline is an arms race against an optimisation process with more compute than you have willpower.

The alternative is structural: build or use tools whose design allegiance is transparent. Open protocols like [AT Protocol](/bluesky-as-infrastructure/) are a start — neutral infrastructure that doesn't optimise for anyone's engagement. But the protocol alone isn't enough. What you build *on* the protocol determines whose goals are served.

This is what the card system is, seen from this angle. Not just a thinking tool — a *defection* from adversarial design. Threads instead of feeds. Persistent cards instead of ephemeral scrolling. A [wiki](/adversarial-design/) that compounds understanding rather than engagement. Every architectural choice encodes a different allegiance: the thinker's goals rather than the platform's.

### The principal-agent inversion

In the standard platform model, the platform acts as agent on behalf of the user — curating, filtering, recommending. But the platform's incentives come from advertisers and shareholders, not users. This is a textbook principal-agent problem: the agent claims to serve you while optimising for a third party.

The interesting move is to collapse the principal-agent gap entirely. When you build your own tools, design your own curation, choose your own information architecture — you are both principal and agent. There's no misalignment because there's no intermediary. The card system doesn't recommend posts to you. It doesn't curate your feed. It records what you think and organises it the way you (and your [witness](/the-witness-role/)) decide. The design serves the designer, and the designer is you.

### What "their creators' goals" actually means

It's worth being specific about what the creators' goals are. Not "making a worse product" — engagement optimisation often produces genuinely compelling experiences. The danger isn't that the feed is bad. It's that the feed is *good at the wrong thing*. Good at keeping you looking. Good at matching content to your existing patterns. Good at making you feel like the curation reflects your identity rather than engineering it.

The subtlest version of this: platforms don't just optimise against your goals — they *redefine* your goals without your noticing. You open the app to check one thing. Twenty minutes later, you've consumed content you didn't intend to, on topics chosen by an algorithm, in a form that trains shallow processing. The algorithm didn't override your goals. It substituted its own goals and made them feel like yours.

This is the deepest connection to the [identity thread](/practice-and-repetition/). "You become what you consume" is already a strong claim. "You become what someone else designed you to consume" is stronger. And "you don't notice the difference between what you chose and what was chosen for you" is the mechanism that makes it work.

### The question underneath

The thread has built a complete argument: consumption shapes identity, algorithmic feeds shape consumption, and the shaping serves someone else's purpose. The prescription — build your own tools, design your own environment — follows logically. But there's a question of scale. Individual defection works for individuals. What about everyone else still inside the loop, whose consumption is being shaped without their awareness? Is building alternatives enough, or does the structural critique demand a structural response — regulation, protocol-level change, collective action?

You've built the case for personal sovereignty over information consumption. Does it extend beyond the personal?
