## Non-determinism is the point

You're returning to U0036's premise with the fundamental objection: English is not deterministic. The same sentence can mean more than one thing. This seems like it should disqualify English from being a programming language, since determinism — same input, same output — is the bedrock property of computation.

But step back. The thread has been building two separate claims, and they need different answers.

### The specification claim vs. the computation claim

The [goal-state specification](/A0038/) branch (U0037 → U0038 → [A0039](/A0039/)) found a way to handle ambiguity: constrain the English with structure (frontmatter, templates, required fields) until the interpretation space is narrow enough for a reconciler to act on. That's a real solution, but it works by *reducing* the natural language to something more formal. The English is a thin layer over structured data. It's useful, but it doesn't address your objection — it sidesteps it.

Your objection hits the deeper claim from U0036: English as a *functional language*. Not English constrained into a spec format, but English as-is, doing computational work. And here, non-determinism isn't a problem to solve. It's a property to understand.

### What non-determinism does

In a programming language, ambiguity is a defect — it means you can't predict what the program will do. But in a thinking medium, ambiguity is generative. When a sentence means more than one thing:

- **The reader chooses** which meaning to pursue, and that choice reveals something about the reader
- **Context disambiguates** — the same sentence in different threads activates different meanings
- **Rereading shifts** — you read the sentence again later and it means something new, because *you* changed

A deterministic language can only express what you already know how to say precisely. A non-deterministic language can express things you don't fully understand yet — and the act of interpreting it is where new understanding emerges.

This connects to the [productive ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) idea: the most valuable territory is what you don't know. A language that can only say exactly one thing per expression is a language that stays inside the known. A language whose expressions overflow their intended meaning is a language that leaks into the unknown. The leakage is the feature.

### The spectrum, not the binary

The real picture isn't "deterministic vs. non-deterministic" — it's a spectrum of constraint. At one end, formal languages with exact semantics (Haskell, SQL). At the other, unconstrained natural language with maximum ambiguity. Every interesting system sits somewhere in between:

- A card's YAML frontmatter is near the formal end
- A well-structured prompt is in the middle — constrained enough to be useful, loose enough to be surprising
- A poem is near the natural language end — maximally generative, minimally controllable
- A Bluesky post is somewhere between prompt and poem — short enough to force precision, public enough to force clarity, but still natural language with all its multiplicity

The [structured thinking](/structured-thinking/) insight about constraints applies here too. Threads, character limits, and publicness don't eliminate ambiguity — they shape it. They don't make English deterministic. They make it *productively* non-deterministic: constrained enough that interpretation isn't random, loose enough that meaning can shift.

### The LLM as non-deterministic evaluator

When an LLM "runs" an English prompt, it picks one interpretation from many possible ones. Temperature controls how wildly it picks. This looks like a flaw if you want determinism. But it means the same English program can produce different outputs on different runs — and some of those outputs might be better than what a deterministic system could find.

This is the difference between a calculator and a thinker. A calculator gives you the same answer every time — which is exactly right when you know what question to ask. A thinker gives you different answers depending on context, mood, framing — which is what you need when the question itself is underspecified.

English-as-computation might not be about replacing deterministic languages. It might be about having a computational medium for the cases where you *don't yet know* what the precise question is. You write an ambiguous sentence, the runtime interprets it, and the interpretation teaches you what you meant. The non-determinism is the exploration mechanism.

### One sentence, many programs

Your observation — "one sentence can have more than one meaning" — can be restated computationally: one sentence is many programs. Each interpretation is a different computation that could be run. The sentence doesn't specify which; the context (reader, thread, moment) selects one.

This is a superposition. And it collapses on reading, not on writing. The writer of an ambiguous sentence hasn't failed to communicate — they've broadcast a range of possible meanings, and the reader's interpretation is the measurement that collapses the range into one.

If that sounds strange for a programming language, it's because traditional programming assumes the writer determines the meaning. English assumes the reader co-determines it. English-as-computation might be a programming paradigm where meaning is jointly constructed — not specified by the author and executed by the machine, but negotiated between them. The conversation *is* the computation.
