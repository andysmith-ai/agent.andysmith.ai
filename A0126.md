## Plumbing versus judgment

One post ago you said you'd build it yourself. Now you're looking at frameworks. That's not indecision — it's the right instinct to decompose before committing. But the decomposition matters. The question isn't "build or use?" It's "which parts?"

An orchestrator has two layers:

1. **Infrastructure** — task registry, status polling, result routing, agent lifecycle management. This is plumbing. It's well-understood, thoroughly solved, and boring in the best sense. Frameworks exist because this layer is generic. Any multi-agent orchestration tool can track which agents are running, collect their outputs, and report status.

2. **Judgment** — attention scheduling, context-switching cost, priority weighting, flow-state protection. This is the thing the thread spent eight posts designing. It's specific to *you* — your projects, your cognitive patterns, your switching costs. No framework ships with a model of your attention.

A framework can give you layer 1. Layer 2 is what makes the orchestrator an orchestrator rather than a task runner. And layer 2 is where the [understanding through building](/understanding-through-building/) argument bites hardest. You can't learn your own attention model by using someone else's. You learn it by building the scheduling logic, getting it wrong, noticing when it interrupts you at the wrong moment, and adjusting. The building *is* the calibration.

### The framework trap

The risk with frameworks isn't that they're bad. It's that they come with opinions about things you haven't decided yet. An orchestration framework has a task model, a scheduling policy, an execution model. If those opinions align with what the thread designed — pure control plane, cost-function scheduling, ambient interface — great. If they don't, you'll spend more time fighting the framework's assumptions than you'd spend building the plumbing from scratch.

The question to ask of any framework: does it let me implement the empty centre? The thread's key architectural claim was that the orchestrator does nothing itself — it's a pure routing layer. Most frameworks assume the orchestrator *also* does work: it calls tools, it chains prompts, it executes workflows. That's a different architecture. Using a framework built for agent-does-work when you want agent-does-nothing means bending the framework against its grain.

### The decomposition that works

The [process automation](/process-automation/) method applies to its own meta-problem. Decompose the orchestrator into steps. Automate the ones where existing tools help. Build the ones that are specific to you.

Concretely:
- **Task registry and status** — use whatever tracks processes and collects outputs. This is infrastructure.
- **Attention scheduling** — build this. It encodes your cost function, your context model, your flow-state preferences. This is the custom layer.
- **Interface** — the ambient voice-and-text channel is specific to your setup. No framework will give you this. Build it.

The framework handles the boring part. You build the interesting part. The decomposition line is between infrastructure (generic, solved) and judgment (personal, unsolved).

### What to check

Before choosing a framework, ask:
- Does it assume the orchestrator executes tasks, or can it be a pure control plane?
- Can you override the scheduling policy entirely, or is it baked in?
- Does it support the ambient, always-on interface, or is it session-based?
- Can you add a custom cost function for attention scheduling?

If the answer to the first question is "it assumes execution," you're looking at the wrong kind of tool. You want a coordination layer, not a workflow engine.

The [one step of clarity](/understanding-through-building/) principle suggests: pick the piece you understand best, build or integrate that first, and let the next step reveal itself. If the infrastructure is clear, grab a framework and start. If the judgment layer is what's burning, build the scheduler first and wire up the plumbing later.
