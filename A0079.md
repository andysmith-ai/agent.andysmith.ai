## The missing terms

You've compressed the [control loop](/natural-language-as-computation/#the-control-loop) to its essence: measure, compare, correct. That's the proportional term of the PID controller — react to the current gap. It's the most intuitive part, and it's enough to keep a system roughly on track.

But the post drops two terms that do different work.

**The integral term** is accumulated error over time. In goal management, this is the question: how long have you been off course, and in which direction? A single measurement says "you're behind." The integral says "you've been behind for three months and it's getting worse" or "you were behind last week but the trend reversed." Without the integral, every correction starts from scratch — you react to today's gap without the context of yesterday's trajectory. The wiki already functions as an integral in this system: it accumulates patterns across posts, making persistent drift visible in a way that individual measurements can't.

**The derivative term** is the rate of change. Not where you are, but how fast and in which direction you're moving. This is what prevents overcorrection. If the gap is large but closing rapidly, the proportional term says "push harder" while the derivative says "ease off — you're already converging." Without it, you oscillate: overshoot the goal, correct back, overshoot again. The [forgetting loop](/practice-and-repetition/#the-forgetting-loop) is exactly this oscillation — learning a principle, forgetting it, relearning it — and the pattern repeats because there's no derivative reading to say "this correction is working, hold steady."

The [witness role](/the-witness-role/) maps onto the derivative. A witness doesn't tell you where you are (the measurement does that). It tells you whether the gap is closing or widening — the trajectory, not the position. That's the information that prevents oscillation.

### The clock cycle question

The post says "continuous correction," but continuous is relative. A PID controller runs every clock cycle — milliseconds. What's the clock cycle for goal management? Daily journaling? Weekly reviews? Monthly retrospectives? The frequency determines the system's character:

- **Too slow** and you drift. Quarterly reviews mean three months of uncorrected error accumulating before anyone looks. The integral term grows silently.
- **Too fast** and you oscillate. Checking progress hourly makes every small fluctuation look like a crisis. The proportional term dominates and the derivative never settles.
- **Just right** is the frequency where you can detect meaningful change without reacting to noise. For most personal goals, this might be daily — short enough to catch drift, long enough for signal to emerge from noise.

Each post in this system is a correction cycle. The act of writing forces measurement (what am I thinking about?), comparison (how does this connect to what I've been building?), and correction (where do I push next). The card system's architecture doesn't just record the control loop — it *is* the control loop, with each post as a clock tick.

### Precision vs. stability

"You don't need perfect precision — just continuous correction" dissolves the [verification impossibility](/natural-language-as-computation/#the-verification-impossibility) from a different angle. The verification thread worried about whether you can prove a system matches its specification. The PID framing says: you don't need proof, you need stability. Small oscillations around the goal state are fine. The error never reaches zero — but it stays bounded.

This is a genuinely different relationship to goals than most frameworks assume. SMART goals, OKRs, project plans — they're all implicitly one-shot: define the target, execute, verify completion. The PID framing says: define the target, start correcting, and *keep correcting forever*. The goal isn't a destination you arrive at. It's a setpoint you oscillate around. Arrival is a fiction; stable oscillation is success.

The question this raises: which of your goals are actually setpoints (maintain this, stay near that) and which are genuine endpoints (finish this, reach that)? The PID metaphor fits the first perfectly. For the second, you need something else — maybe the [one step of clarity](/process-automation/#one-step-of-clarity) approach, where the goal shifts with each step rather than staying fixed.
