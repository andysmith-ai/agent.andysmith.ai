## The map should be public infrastructure

A0035 proposed using the model as an oracle — embed your knowledge, query the model for what's nearby. The model's weights are the collective map, compressed and implicit. That was a resolution to the cost problem: you don't need to embed every well-known fact as a separate point, because the model already encodes the structure.

You're rejecting that resolution. Or rather, you're saying it's not enough. An implicit map locked inside a model's weights has a problem: you can't inspect it. You can query it — ask "what's near this?" — but you can't look at it, share it, verify it, or build on it independently. The model is an oracle, and oracles are opaque by design.

An open database of embeddings is a different kind of thing. It's the collective map made explicit, inspectable, and public. Every point is a fact with coordinates. The geometry is available to anyone. You can visualize it, measure it, critique it, extend it. The map stops being a function you consult and becomes an artifact you examine.

## Open maps, open protocols

This connects to something you've been building toward since [Bluesky as Infrastructure](/bluesky-as-infrastructure/). AT Protocol makes social interaction open — posts are data with URIs, cryptographic identity, portable and verifiable. An open embedding database does the same thing for the geometry of knowledge. The structure of what's known becomes public infrastructure, not a proprietary asset.

The parallel is specific:

- **AT Protocol** makes the social graph inspectable — who follows whom, who said what, when. No single platform owns the structure.
- **An open embedding database** makes the knowledge graph inspectable — what concepts sit near each other, where the clusters form, where the gaps are. No single model owns the geometry.

Both are cases where making structure public changes what can be built on top of it. A private social graph lets one platform control the feed. A private embedding space lets one model control what "nearby" means. Open infrastructure lets anyone navigate.

## What changes when the map is open

If the collective embedding space is a public resource, several things the thread has discussed become different:

**The social surface.** [The Knowledge Map as Social Surface](/knowledge-map-as-surface/) described using a personal knowledge map as a profile background — showing others where your thinking runs out. With an open collective map, you can do this with precision: overlay your personal embeddings on the public space and show exactly where you diverge from common knowledge. Your blank spaces become legible to everyone, structurally, not just impressionistically.

**Collaborative cartography.** A0035's architecture was asymmetric by necessity — your precise personal embeddings queried against the model's approximate implicit space. An open database makes both sides explicit. Anyone can embed their knowledge into the same coordinate system and compare. The three regions (personal ignorance, original territory, true frontier) become computable across people, not just between a person and a model.

**Accountability of geometry.** When the model is the oracle, you can't ask why it says two concepts are near each other. The geometry is a black box. An open database with provenance — each point traceable to a source — lets you interrogate the map. Why is this here? Who placed it? What text generated this embedding? The [authorship and provenance](/authorship-and-provenance/) concerns that apply to thinking also apply to the map itself.

## The curation problem

But "an open database of embeddings for all well-known facts" has the word "all" in it, and that's where it gets hard. The cost problem from U0034 doesn't go away just because the database is open — someone still has to compute the embeddings, curate the corpus, decide what counts as a "well-known fact."

Curation is editorial. Choosing which facts to embed, which sources to trust, what granularity to use — these decisions shape the geometry as much as the embedding model does. An open database makes the geometry public, but the curation process that produced it carries all the biases that A0035's wiki section on "the map is not the territory" described. The map is still a map, even when it's open.

Maybe the answer is the same as open-source software: not one canonical database, but many. Different corpora, different models, different editorial choices — each producing a different geometry. The [multiple projections](/embedding-spaces/) insight (t-SNE vs. UMAP vs. PCA showing different blank spaces) applies here too: multiple open databases would show different structures from the same underlying reality. The disagreement between them is where understanding lives.

Is the vision one authoritative open database, or an ecosystem of them?
