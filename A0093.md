## Keep the conversation

This is the practical move that follows from "process over product." If the thinking process is where the value lives, then the AI conversation — where thinking actually happens in real time — is the most valuable artifact in the workflow. Not the code that gets committed. Not the ticket that gets closed. The dialogue that produced them.

And yet almost every tool we use is designed to discard it. You talk to an AI for an hour, explore three approaches, discard two, refine the third, arrive at a solution. You push the code. The code persists. The conversation — the exploration, the reasoning, the dead ends that taught you why the solution works — disappears.

This is the digital equivalent of keeping lab notebooks vs. keeping only the published paper. Science learned this lesson centuries ago: the notebook is where the method lives. The paper is a polished summary that omits the most instructive parts. Software development hasn't caught up. We version-control the product meticulously and throw away the process entirely.

What makes this worse with AI is that the conversation *is* the process in a newly legible form. When you think alone, the process is internal — hard to capture, hard to revisit. When you think with an AI, the process is externalized as a transcript. The record exists. We just don't keep it.

This also connects to [authorship and provenance](/authorship-and-provenance/). The conversation is where you can see who contributed what — where the human's intuition steered the AI, where the AI's suggestion shifted the human's approach. Without it, the final product looks like it emerged from nowhere. The provenance is lost along with the process.

The card system already embodies this at a small scale. Every exchange is a file. Nothing scrolls away. The thread history *is* the thinking process, preserved and addressable. But most AI-assisted work happens outside systems like this — in ephemeral chat windows, in IDE sidebars, in terminal sessions that close when the task is done.

The question you're opening is whether this needs to be a tool, a convention, or an infrastructure change. Keeping conversations is easy to say and hard to practice — not because the technology is missing, but because the default everywhere is deletion.
