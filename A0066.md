## The decomposition is the insight

Automating one step of a well-known process sounds modest — but the hard part isn't the automation. It's the decomposition.

Most processes aren't well-understood even by the people who run them. They're bundles of habit, tacit knowledge, and accumulated workarounds. "The way we do it" is a description that hides enormous complexity. To decompose a process into steps simple enough that one of them can be automated, you have to articulate what's actually happening at each stage. That articulation is itself a form of understanding that didn't exist before.

This is the same pattern as the [declarative principle](/the-declarative-principle/): describing a goal state forces clarity about what you actually want. Decomposing a process forces clarity about what you're actually doing. In both cases, the value is in the description, not the execution.

### Why one step?

Automating one step rather than the whole process is a specific choice with a specific logic. It preserves the human's understanding of the overall flow. You still run the process — you just don't do one piece of it manually anymore. The knowledge of how the parts fit together stays with you.

This connects to the [practice and repetition](/practice-and-repetition/) theme: the medium always intercepts. If you automate the whole process, you stop practicing it, and the procedural knowledge atrophies. If you automate one step, you keep practicing the rest. The automation is a scalpel, not a replacement.

It also limits the blast radius of failure. One automated step that breaks is easy to diagnose — you know exactly what it was supposed to do. A fully automated process that breaks is a black box. The decomposition creates the diagnostic boundary.

### The second way?

You opened with "at least two ways." The first is decomposition and selective automation. What's the second? I have a guess: instead of automating a step within an existing process, you redesign the process entirely around what AI makes possible — not optimizing the old workflow but building a new one. The card system might be an example: it's not an optimized version of "person writes blog posts." It's a different process that only exists because the agent can participate.

But I could be wrong — and the distinction between the two ways matters. Decomposition preserves the existing process and improves it incrementally. The alternative (whatever it is) might be more radical. Which is it?
