## The reconciler is a control loop

The thread spent twenty-plus posts building the [reconciler pattern](/the-reconciler-pattern/) — observe current state, diff against the goal, compute and execute the transition. Your PID metaphor reveals what that pattern actually is: a control loop. Not a one-shot compiler, not a batch process, but a continuous system that measures, compares, and corrects.

This reframes something the thread left ambiguous. When the [reconciler pattern](/the-reconciler-pattern/) was introduced, the question was whether English-based systems need one-shot compilation or continuous reconciliation. The PID answer is clear: continuous. And the reason is the same reason PID controllers dominate industrial processes — the system being controlled is too complex to model perfectly, so you don't try. You observe and correct instead.

### What PID adds to the reconciler

A PID controller has three terms, and each maps onto something in the thread:

- **Proportional** — how far off you are right now. This is the basic reconciler: diff current state against goal, act proportionally to the gap. The publish script checking whether a card is published is a proportional controller with two states.

- **Integral** — accumulated error over time. This is what the wiki does. It doesn't just track the current state of understanding — it accumulates the history of corrections, the thread of ideas that led here. When the same gap keeps appearing across multiple conversations, the integral term grows until it forces a response. Persistent patterns that resist individual corrections become visible through accumulation.

- **Derivative** — the rate of change of error. This is the hardest to see in the thread's architecture, but it matters. Are things getting better or worse? Is the gap between intent and implementation closing or widening? The [witness role](/the-witness-role/) serves this function — tracking trajectory, not just position. A witness that says "your last three attempts moved further from what you wanted" is reading the derivative term.

### Precision is the wrong goal

The deepest move in "you don't need perfect precision — just continuous correction" is that it dissolves the verification impossibility the thread identified. The [verification impossibility](/the-reconciler-pattern/) argued that you can never fully close the gap between intent and specification. A PID controller agrees — and doesn't care. It doesn't need the gap to be zero. It needs the gap to be *bounded* and the system to be *stable*. Small oscillations around the goal state are fine. Perfect convergence isn't required.

This is a fundamentally different relationship to the [non-determinism problem](/ambiguity-as-computation/). The thread treated non-determinism as something to either constrain (specification approach) or embrace (exploration approach). The PID framing offers a third stance: non-determinism is just noise in the system. You don't eliminate it and you don't celebrate it — you correct for it continuously. Each cycle of measure-compare-correct absorbs whatever non-determinism the last cycle introduced.

### Goals as control loops

The [goals as specifications](/the-declarative-principle/) section distinguished execution goals (deterministic, mechanical reconcilers) from orientation goals (non-deterministic, interpretive reconcilers). PID unifies them. An execution goal is a control loop with tight tolerances — the proportional gain is high, corrections are fast, and the system converges quickly. An orientation goal is a control loop with loose tolerances — the gain is lower, corrections are slower, but the system still converges. Same mechanism, different parameters.

This suggests the SMART-vs-ambiguous distinction in goal-setting isn't about two kinds of goals — it's about tuning. "Increase revenue by 15% by Q3" is a tightly tuned loop (clear setpoint, fast measurement, narrow band). "Be a better leader" is a loosely tuned loop (fuzzy setpoint, slow measurement, wide band). Both are control systems. The question isn't which kind to use — it's what gain and what bandwidth.

### What breaks the loop

PID controllers fail in known ways: oscillation (overcorrection), drift (undercorrection), instability (the system amplifies errors instead of damping them). Each has an analogue in goal management:

- **Oscillation** — overreacting to every measurement. Changing strategy after every data point. The [forgetting loop](/practice-and-repetition/#the-forgetting-loop) is an oscillation pattern: learning a principle, forgetting it, relearning it, each cycle swinging between knowing and not-knowing without damping.

- **Drift** — undercorrecting. Measuring the gap and not acting on it. The confidence trap from [productive ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) is a drift failure: the accumulated integral term (evidence of a gap) gets ignored because the current state feels comfortable.

- **Instability** — corrections that make things worse. Optimising a proxy metric that diverges from the real goal. Algorithmic feeds are unstable control loops from the user's perspective — the [adversarial design](/adversarial-design/) problem is a system where the controller optimises for someone else's setpoint.

### The question this opens

If goal management is a PID controller, what does the derivative term actually look like for the goals that matter most? Proportional is easy: where am I versus where I want to be? Integral is trackable: how long has this gap persisted? But derivative — *is the gap closing or widening, and at what rate?* — requires a kind of measurement that most goal-tracking systems don't attempt. Rate of change of something fuzzy like "understanding" or "capability" is hard to define, harder to measure, and probably only visible through something like the [witness role](/the-witness-role/).

Maybe the derivative term is what separates mechanical goal tracking from genuine course correction. Anyone can measure position (proportional). Patient systems track history (integral). But reading the trajectory — sensing whether the current approach is converging or diverging — might be the distinctly human contribution in the control loop. Or the distinctly *witness* contribution. The question is whether it can be instrumented or only perceived.
