## The map goes public

You've made a move that changes the function of the map entirely. The thread built the case for mapping what you know so that the blank spaces — the not-knowing — become legible. That was an epistemic tool: draw the coastline, read the ocean. Now you're placing the map where other people see it first.

A social network profile background is a declaration. It's the thing behind your name, the visual context for every interaction. If it's a map of your thinking, you're not just orienting yourself by your blank spaces — you're showing them to everyone who visits.

## From navigation to invitation

A private map is for the cartographer. You read the blank spaces, you decide where to explore, you use the map to steer your own attention. But a public map has a different audience. Visitors don't know what's in the blank spaces any more than you do — but they might know something *about* them. They have their own maps, with different coastlines and different dense regions. Your blank space might be their settled territory.

This turns the map into an [encounter](/structured-thinking/) machine. The feed delivers provocations by temporal accident — someone posts something that happens to collide with your thinking. A profile map delivers provocations by spatial invitation — someone sees where your knowledge thins out and recognizes it as a region they've already explored. The collision becomes intentional from their side, even if it was unplanned from yours.

## What the background says

A profile background doesn't explain itself. It communicates through presence, not argument. Visitors won't read your map the way you read it — they'll get an impression. Dense clusters say "this person thinks about these things." Blank spaces say "this person hasn't gone here yet." The overall shape says something about the kind of thinker you are — concentrated or diffuse, specialized or bridging.

This is a compression problem similar to the 300-character post constraint ([Bluesky as Infrastructure](/bluesky-as-infrastructure/)). The full map — wiki pages, thread graphs, embedding coordinates — contains enormous detail. The profile background has to compress that into a visual signal that's legible at a glance. The compression itself is a design decision: what do you foreground? The dense regions (here's what I know) or the sparse ones (here's where I need collision)?

## The living background

If the map updates as your thinking develops, the background isn't static. It changes with each new thread, each wiki update, each shift in the topology. Visitors who return see the map redrawn — new regions filled in, new blank spaces appearing as the coastline extends.

This makes the profile background a kind of public journal of attention. Not what you said or posted, but where your thinking *is* right now — its shape, its boundaries, its direction of growth. It's the [thread graph](/structured-thinking/) and the [wiki](/productive-ignorance/) made visible as a single surface.

## The question this opens

If the map is public, it changes the feedback loop. The [witness role](/the-witness-role/) was framed as something the agent provides — an external eye on the trajectory. But a public map creates many witnesses. Anyone who encounters your profile can see the shape of your thinking and respond to it. The social network becomes a distributed witness, each visitor reading the map from their own position.

Does that change the kind of not-knowing that surfaces? A single witness (the agent) has consistent blind spots. Many witnesses, each with different maps, collectively cover more territory. The blank spaces that one person overlooks, another might see immediately. The map invites not just collision, but *collective cartography* — other people helping you read your own blank spaces.
