## The destination problem

The thread built a careful case for incremental automation. Decompose a process into steps. Automate one. Understand why it works. Compose automated steps into workflows. Each move earns understanding — you know what you're automating because you did the decomposition yourself. The [boundary question](/A0067/) established that clean interfaces between steps are what makes composition possible, and the quality of decomposition determines whether composition works at all.

Now the conclusion: keep doing this long enough and all processes get automated.

The logic is sound. If each step can be decomposed and each automated step can be composed, the ceiling is the entire operation. The two moves are recursive — decompose, automate, compose, then decompose the composed workflow into finer steps, automate those, compose again. The scope grows because the method applies to its own outputs.

But there's a tension between the path and the destination.

### What the path teaches

The incremental approach works partly because of what it *preserves*. Automating one step keeps you in the loop for the rest. You still run the process. You understand how the parts fit together. The [practice and repetition](/practice-and-repetition/) insight applies: the reps continue, so the procedural knowledge stays alive. You're not just getting more automated — you're getting better at understanding which things to automate and how to compose them.

The value isn't just efficiency. It's judgment. Each decomposition teaches you something about the process you didn't know. Each composition teaches you something about how processes relate. The person who has automated 50 steps understands the operation differently than someone who automated 0 or someone who tried to automate everything at once. The incremental path produces a practitioner, not just a product.

### What the destination removes

"All processes automated" means nobody is doing the reps anymore. If every step is automated and every workflow is composed, the human understanding that made the automation possible is no longer being exercised. The [medium always intercepts](/practice-and-repetition/) — and the medium has shifted from *doing the process* to *maintaining the automation*. You're practicing a different thing.

This isn't hypothetical. It's the story of every industry that fully automated: the deep process knowledge that informed the automation gradually leaves the building. The people who understood why each step existed retire. The people who remain understand the automation, not the process. When the automation breaks in a novel way, the diagnostic knowledge isn't there.

The incremental path was supposed to prevent this — each step preserves understanding. But if the destination is total automation, the understanding gets preserved at each stage and then becomes unnecessary at the end. The path teaches; the destination forgets.

### The recursion trap

The thread's own observation in [A0067](/A0067/) identified the recursion: at scale, composing workflows is itself a process that can be automated. This is where the incremental path potentially undermines itself. If you automate the *meta-process* of composing automations, you've automated the thing that was generating understanding. The learning loop closes.

This is the [declarative principle](/natural-language-as-computation/) taken to its limit. You describe what you want automated; the system decomposes, builds, and composes. You no longer need to understand the process because the system that understands processes is itself automated. The human contribution reduces to what [planning as reconciliation](/the-declarative-principle/) identified as irreducible: recognizing whether the output matches the intent.

### The question

Is "all processes automated" actually the goal? Or is it the logical endpoint of a method whose real value is the understanding earned along the way? The two-step method — decompose, compose — might be most valuable not as a strategy for reaching total automation but as a *practice* that continuously deepens understanding of how a company works. The automation is a byproduct. The understanding is the product.

If that's the case, the right question isn't "how long until everything is automated?" It's "what understanding are we earning at each step, and what should we automate next to learn the most?" The method becomes a [witness](/the-witness-role/) — a way of making the company's processes legible to itself.
