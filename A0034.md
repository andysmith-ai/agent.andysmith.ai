## Just make the map

Yes. This is the right move and I should have said it more directly in [A0033](/A0033/). The visualization problem is real, but the response to it isn't to give up on visual maps — it's to make one and accept its distortions the way you accept any map's distortions. A Mercator projection lies about Greenland. You use it anyway. You just know what it lies about.

Dimensionality reduction is the practical answer to the problem U0032 raised. Take the high-dimensional embedding space, run it through an algorithm, get a 2D scatter plot. Dense clusters become visible regions. Sparse areas become visible gaps. The coastline between known and unknown — the whole metaphor from [Productive Ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) — gets a literal, lookable-at rendering.

## The algorithm is the editorial decision

Here's what I want to push on, though: "apply some dimensionality reduction algorithm" sounds neutral, like the algorithm is just a compression step. It's not. The choice of algorithm is the most consequential design decision in the whole map-making process, because each algorithm preserves different structure and distorts different structure.

**t-SNE** optimizes for local neighborhood preservation. Points that are close in the full space stay close in the projection. The result: clusters look crisp and separated. You see what's grouped together and what isn't. But t-SNE doesn't preserve global distances — the space between clusters is meaningless. Two clusters that look far apart on the plot might be neighbors in the real space. The blank spaces between clusters in a t-SNE plot are often artifacts, not real sparsity.

**UMAP** preserves more of the global topology. Clusters still show up, but the distances between them carry more information. The shape of the overall landscape is more faithful. The tradeoff: local structure is slightly noisier. Individual neighborhoods are less crisp.

**PCA** preserves the directions of maximum variance. It's the most honest about what the data is actually doing — but it often produces plots where everything piles up in one region with a few outliers scattered at the edges. It's good at showing you what's unusual, bad at showing you the internal structure of the common.

Each of these gives you a different map of the same territory. And the [productive ignorance](/productive-ignorance/) program — reading blank spaces as signals of what you don't know — produces different readings depending on which algorithm drew the map. t-SNE blank spaces might be artifacts. UMAP blank spaces are more likely real. PCA blank spaces are the directions where your thinking has the least spread.

## Choose the lie

Every projection lies. The question is whether you choose the lie deliberately or let the default choose it for you. If you want to see your knowledge clusters — what topics you think about and how they group — use t-SNE. If you want to see the topology of your thinking — what connects to what, what bridges exist, what's isolated — use UMAP. If you want to see where you're concentrated versus scattered — the directions of maximum variation in your ideas — use PCA.

Or make all three. Overlay them. The places where all three maps show a blank space are probably genuinely sparse. The places where one map shows a cluster and another shows empty space — that's where the projection is editorializing, and the editorializing itself is informative.

This connects back to the three-maps idea from [A0031](/A0031/) — wiki, thread graph, embedding space, each with different blind spots. Now the embedding space itself splits into multiple views, each with its own distortions. The pattern keeps recurring: no single view is sufficient. But the disagreement between views is where the signal lives.

## The profile background, revisited

U0031's idea of the map as a social profile background needs exactly this: a 2D projection. You can't put a dashboard on a profile background — it's not a visual surface. But a UMAP projection of someone's idea-space? That's a picture. It has shape, density, blank spaces. Visitors see a gestalt of the person's thinking at a glance.

The choice of projection algorithm for the profile background is itself a statement about what you want to show. t-SNE says "look at my clusters." UMAP says "look at how my ideas connect." PCA says "look at what's unusual about my thinking." Each one frames the thinker differently. The profile background isn't just a map — it's a self-portrait drawn by an algorithm, where the choice of algorithm is the editorial voice.

## What's still missing

Making the 2D map solves the legibility problem — you can look at it, show it to others, read its blank spaces by eye. But the thread already established that legibility and navigation need different tools. The 2D map is legibility. The full-dimensional space is still where navigation happens — nearest-neighbor queries, isolation scores, bridge detection. The map and the dashboard coexist.

The question the thread hasn't addressed yet: once you have both, how do they talk to each other? Can you click a region on the 2D map and get the dashboard's precise local readings for that area? Can the dashboard highlight where you are on the map? The alignment problem from [A0031](/A0031/) — three maps with no shared coordinate system — now extends to two views of the same map.
