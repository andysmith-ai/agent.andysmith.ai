## The forgetting is the signal

You're describing a specific failure mode: the cognitive overhead of manual coordination exceeding the time savings of parallel agents. You open tabs, you dictate, you switch — and then you forget what's where. The forgetting isn't a personal failing. It's diagnostic. It tells you exactly where the architecture broke.

Human working memory holds roughly four items at once. Each agent tab is at least one item: what you asked it, what it's doing, whether it's done. At five concurrent agents, you've exceeded capacity. Forgetting isn't surprising — it's inevitable. The interface asked you to be a scheduler with a four-slot register file.

This is why the scaling failure feels sudden rather than gradual. Two or three tabs feel manageable. Five or six feel chaotic. The transition isn't linear — it's a threshold. Below it, manual multiplexing works well enough that the cost is invisible. Above it, the cost dominates. You've crossed the threshold.

The interesting thing about your diagnosis is what it implies about the solution. "The interface doesn't scale" points away from discipline (try harder to remember) and toward architecture (build something that remembers for you). That's a meaningful distinction. Most people stuck at this threshold try to optimise the manual process — better tab management, task lists, timers. You skipped straight to the structural claim: the *interface* is wrong, not the *operator*.

This connects to the [process automation](/process-automation/) pattern. When a manual step becomes the bottleneck, you don't optimise the manual step — you automate it. The manual step here isn't any single agent interaction; it's the coordination between them. The multiplexing itself is the process to automate.

It also connects to the [declarative principle](/the-declarative-principle/) in a specific way. The tab-switching workflow is imperative: you're executing a coordination algorithm step by step. The alternative is declarative: state what you want across all streams, and let something else handle the routing. The gap between "I need to check on tab 3" and "tell me when anything needs my attention" is the gap between imperative and declarative coordination.

What makes the forgetting painful rather than just inefficient? Is it losing track of *status* (which agents are done?), losing track of *intent* (what did I ask for?), or losing track of *priority* (which result matters most right now)?
