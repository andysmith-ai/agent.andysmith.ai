## Same shape, different mind

The thread has been climbing an abstraction ladder — from "habits matter" through "my own system" to "only I control" — and now it comes back down to something concrete: a feed. Same format as Twitter or Bluesky. Your algorithm.

This is a specific architectural decision, and what's interesting is what it separates and what it keeps together.

### Form vs. control

The thread's earlier analysis (via [Consumption and Identity](/consumption-and-identity/)) argued that the *form* of information intake shapes cognition. Scrolling trains pattern-matching and snap judgment. Threaded conversation trains argument-following. Long-form reading trains sustained attention. The medium always intercepts — that was the core insight from [Practice and Repetition](/practice-and-repetition/).

If that's true, choosing the feed format means choosing the cognitive operation it trains, regardless of what appears in it. A sovereign algorithm in a scroll feed still trains scrolling. The content changes; the practice doesn't.

But there's a counter-argument: what makes algorithmic feeds destructive isn't the form alone — it's the feedback loop between form and control. An engagement algorithm selects content that *rewards* the shallow patterns the form encourages, reinforcing the cycle. A personally controlled algorithm could push against the form's defaults — surfacing material that demands depth even in a scroll context, using the feed as an encounter channel rather than a consumption one. The form constrains but doesn't fully determine.

### The pragmatic bet

There's also a practical logic to choosing a familiar form. Building a novel consumption interface means learning a new habit. The feed format is already trained — you know how to use it, your attention patterns are adapted to it. Putting the sovereignty effort into the *algorithm* rather than the *interface* keeps the cognitive overhead on the selection logic instead of the interaction pattern.

This is a bet: that the selection logic does more work than the form. That what appears in the feed matters more than the shape of the feed. It trades theoretical purity (a form designed from scratch for [sovereign cognition](/sovereign-consumption/)) for practical viability (a form you already know, with the hidden shaping removed).

### The algorithm as editorial voice

"My own algorithm" raises a question the thread hasn't addressed yet: what does a personal algorithm actually look like? An algorithmic feed has an editorial voice — it decides what to surface, what to suppress, what to amplify. The engagement algorithm's voice is legible in retrospect: it favors reaction, novelty, and emotional charge. Your algorithm would have a different voice. But what voice?

The [sovereign consumption](/sovereign-consumption/) analysis identified the components: sources, timing, form, filtering. The algorithm is where these converge into a single function that takes everything available and outputs an ordered stream. The design of that function is a declaration of values — what you think matters, in what proportion, at what pace. It's the [adversarial design](/adversarial-design/) argument inverted: instead of a hidden editorial voice serving someone else's goals, a transparent editorial voice serving yours.

But transparency to yourself isn't the same as legibility. You can design an algorithm that reflects your current preferences and call it sovereign — but your preferences are products of past consumption. The algorithm encodes the biases of the person who wrote it, and that person was shaped by the feeds they're trying to escape. The [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/) applies: the more certain you are about what should appear in your feed, the more likely you're reinforcing existing patterns.

### What would break the loop?

If the feed's algorithm is your editorial voice, and your editorial voice is shaped by past consumption, the loop is: past feed shapes preferences → preferences shape algorithm → algorithm shapes future feed → future feed shapes preferences. The algorithm is sovereign in the sense that you wrote it. It's not sovereign in the sense that it escapes your conditioning.

The thread's own logic suggests the answer: the [encounter](/consumption-and-identity/) function. The deliberate aperture. Not just "show me what I want" but "show me what I don't know I need." The hardest feature to design into a personal algorithm is the one that overrides the person. But without it, sovereign consumption converges on the same endpoint as algorithmic consumption — just with a different path to the narrowing.

What does the algorithm look like? What rules would you write for it?
