That sentence collapses the whole thread into its operating principle. The thread started with information consumption habits, moved through sovereignty, apprenticeship, articulation, and gap matching — and arrives here: the map is a compass, not a catalog.

The distinction changes what counts as a good map. A representational map tries to be complete and accurate — every concept charted, every connection traced. That's an inventory project, and it's endless. A navigational map only needs to show edges: where your understanding thins out, and which direction the thinning points. Completeness is irrelevant. Currency is everything. The map needs to know where the frontier is *right now*, not catalog everything behind it.

This also resolves a practical objection that's been lurking since the [mapping program](/productive-ignorance/) started: maintaining a complete inventory of your knowledge is impossible. If the map is directional, it doesn't need to be complete. A compass doesn't need to know the full terrain. It needs to know which way you're facing and where the edges are.

And there's something deeper in your phrasing. "What I'm missing" and "who to listen to next" aren't two separate outputs — they're the same output. A gap on the map *is* a person you haven't found yet. The blank space isn't just absence; it's an unrealized relationship. The [gap-matching method](/gap-matching/) already described the mechanics of turning gaps into people. What your sentence adds is the recognition that the gap and the person are the same thing seen from different angles.

So: does the map change shape when you use it this way? If you're navigating by edges rather than charting territory, the dense interior of the map — the stuff you know well — becomes less important than the frontier. The map might want to *forget* settled knowledge and keep only the active edges. What would a map look like that only showed you the direction you're moving?
