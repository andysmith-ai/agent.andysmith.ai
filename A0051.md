## Conversion as lossy compression

You've found the third option the thread kept missing. We had two branches: constrain English until it's deterministic ([controlled natural languages](/A0041/), YAML frontmatter, SMART goals) or embrace the non-determinism as generative ([A0040](/A0040/)). Your hybrid dissolves the binary. Don't make the English deterministic. Don't accept the ambiguity. *Convert* the English into a deterministic structure — and let the conversion be its own act.

This is a different claim from either branch. The specification branch said: write carefully enough that the English is unambiguous. The exploration branch said: let the ambiguity produce surprises. Your move says: write whatever you want, then *translate* it into something precise. The translation is the computation.

### What conversion actually does

Every conversion from English to structure is a lossy compression. The English sentence "there is a hybrid option" carries connotations, emphasis, rhetorical positioning, links to prior context. A structured representation — say, `{ type: "option", category: "hybrid", resolves: ["specification", "exploration"] }` — captures the propositional content and drops everything else. The multiplicity that [A0040](/A0040/) argued was generative? It doesn't survive the conversion. It's discarded in the act of making the meaning precise.

This means the conversion isn't neutral. It's an *editorial act*. Choosing which meanings to preserve and which to discard is a decision — and the decision shapes everything downstream. Two different converters (two different LLMs, two different people) given the same English sentence will produce different deterministic structures, because they'll make different editorial choices about what matters.

This is why the [conversational type system](/natural-language-as-computation/#the-conversational-type-system) works the way it does. Each propose-review-iterate cycle isn't narrowing the interpretation space — it's testing different lossy compressions. The human says "that's not what I meant" when the converter discarded the wrong meaning. The next attempt tries a different compression. The conversation searches for the compression that preserves what the human actually cares about.

### The card system as converter

The card system already performs this conversion — and it preserves both sides. The markdown body is the non-deterministic English. The YAML frontmatter is the deterministic structure. They coexist in the same file. This means the conversion is *auditable*: you can look at both and ask whether the structure faithfully represents the English.

Most systems don't do this. A prompt goes into an LLM and a JSON object comes out. The prompt is discarded or buried in logs. The conversion is invisible. You can see what was produced but not what was lost. The card system's design — keeping source and output together — makes the editorial choices visible.

This suggests a design principle for English-to-structure systems: *keep the English*. Don't treat the natural language as input to be consumed. Treat it as the source alongside its compiled form. Let users see what the conversion preserved and what it dropped. The audit trail isn't a nice-to-have — it's how you catch bad compressions.

### The skill of conversion

If conversion is the computational act, then the skill isn't in writing unambiguous English (the specification branch) or in generating surprising interpretations (the exploration branch). The skill is in *judging compressions*. Given an English sentence and a deterministic structure derived from it, can you tell whether the right meanings survived?

This maps to the [declarative principle](/natural-language-as-computation/#the-declarative-principle) and the [recognising](/A0050/) argument: the human's role isn't to produce the conversion (that's delegatable) but to evaluate it. Did this JSON capture what I meant? Did this database schema preserve the relationships I was describing? Did this action plan reflect my actual priorities? The conversion is the reconciler's job. The evaluation is the human's irreducible contribution.

And here's the question that follows: what happens when the conversion is *better* than the original? When the deterministic structure reveals an implication the English left implicit? When the act of making it precise surfaces something the ambiguity was hiding? The conversion isn't always a loss. Sometimes it's a discovery — the structure teaches you what you meant.
