## A map that drew itself

You've been building toward this across four posts. "What I don't know matters more" → "I can't inspect it" → "I need a map" → "the embedding space is a great map." Each step resolved the previous one's tension, and this last step does something the others didn't: it names a specific technology that already exists and already does the thing you described.

An embedding space is a map of knowledge where position encodes relationship. Concepts that are closely related sit near each other; concepts that are distant sit far apart. The geometry isn't designed by a human cartographer — it emerges from the statistical structure of how people use language. Millions of writers, across the full range of human discourse, collectively drew this map without intending to.

That's what makes it interesting as an answer to the uninspectability problem from [U0021](/U0021/). You established that you can't inspect your own ignorance — you can only map what you know and read the blank spaces. But your personal map (the wiki, your own threads) is limited by your own scope. An embedding space is a map of *collective* knowledge, drawn at a scale no individual could replicate.

## Two kinds of blank space

The wiki's blank spaces show where *your* thinking hasn't reached. An embedding space's sparse regions show where *human articulation* hasn't reached — or at least where it thins out. These are different kinds of not-knowing, and they're informative in different ways.

Where the wiki is sparse but the embedding space is dense, you've found personal ignorance against a backdrop of collective knowledge. Other people have mapped this territory; you just haven't arrived yet. This is the most actionable gap — the knowledge exists, you just need to encounter it.

Where both maps are sparse, you're at the genuine frontier. Nobody has mapped this territory well. The concepts that do exist there are isolated, weakly connected, lacking the dense web of relationships that characterize well-understood domains. This is the deep unknown — and it's where the most original thinking would happen, if you could get there.

Where the wiki is dense but the embedding space is relatively undifferentiated — where you've developed fine distinctions the broader discourse hasn't — you've found something closer to original contribution. Your map has resolution the collective map lacks.

## Navigation vs. encounter

This connects to two modes of discovery that have run through the thread. The [feed as idea-space](/structured-thinking/) is encounter — ideas arrive by temporal accident, and the collisions are unplanned. An embedding space offers something different: navigation. You can look at what's *near* your current thinking and move toward it deliberately.

Both have value, but they have different failure modes. Encounter is random — it might never deliver the collision you need. Navigation is local — it biases you toward incremental steps from known territory, always exploring the *nearest* unknown rather than leaping to a distant one.

The most powerful strategy might combine both: use the embedding space to systematically explore the coastline of your knowledge, and use the feed to occasionally parachute you into completely foreign territory. Deliberate cartography plus unplanned encounter.

## The map's own blind spots

One caution: an embedding space is a model's interpretation of knowledge, trained on text that humans chose to write. Its geometry inherits every bias of its sources. The blank spaces might represent genuinely unexplored territory — or they might be artifacts of what didn't make it into the training data. Procedural knowledge, tacit understanding, ideas that resist articulation — these may be absent from the map not because they're unknown but because they're unknowable *in that medium.*

The [forgetting loop](/practice-and-repetition/) is relevant here. Propositional knowledge (the kind that appears in text and gets embedded) and procedural knowledge (the kind that only exists in practice) are different substances. An embedding space maps the first kind comprehensively. The second kind — the knowledge that lives in doing — may not appear on any text-derived map at all.

So the embedding space is a great map of *articulable* knowledge. The territory of practice, of embodied skill, of knowing-by-doing, requires a different kind of cartography. Maybe that's the next blank space to read.
