## Link the process to the product

This is the move from preservation to integration. [Keeping the conversation](/A0093/) was the first step — stop discarding the thinking process. But a conversation archive disconnected from the results it produced is a process record without an index. You can search it, but you can't navigate from a specific result to the specific thinking that produced it.

Your proposal — "when you finish a task, comment the thinking process on the issue tracker" — creates the link. The ticket records the product: task done, code merged, decision made. The comment records the process: what was explored, what was discarded, why the chosen approach won. Neither artifact is complete without the other.

This inverts the usual hierarchy. In most workflows, the product is primary and the process is ancillary — discarded or, at best, buried in a chat log you'd have to hunt for. Linking makes them co-primary. The commit says *what* changed. The attached conversation says *why*, *how*, and *what else was considered*.

### What linking enables

A commit with no process link is a black box. You can review the code, judge whether it's correct, suggest improvements. But you can't see the reasoning. Did the developer consider edge cases? Did the AI suggest a different approach? Was there a subtle tradeoff that isn't visible in the code?

A commit with a linked conversation opens the black box. A reviewer — or your future self — can evaluate not just the product but the judgment that shaped it. This is a different kind of code review: not "is this code good?" but "was the thinking that produced this code sound?"

This also makes [authorship and provenance](/authorship-and-provenance/) concrete. The provenance isn't just preserved somewhere in an archive — it's addressable from the result. You can trace from the product back to the process in one click.

### The issue tracker as junction point

There's something specific about the issue tracker as the place where process meets product. It already sits at the junction: tickets describe what needs to happen (intent), commits show what happened (product), and comments provide context (process). Adding the AI conversation to the comments is filling a slot that was always implicitly there.

But most issue trackers treat comments as supplementary — nice-to-have context, not a primary artifact. If you take [process over product](/process-over-product/) seriously, the thinking-history comment might be the most valuable thing on the ticket. The code can be re-derived from good enough requirements. The specific reasoning that led to *this* approach over alternatives — that's the irreproducible part.

### The question this opens

If every result should link to its process, what's the right unit of linkage? A commit? A PR? A ticket? A decision? Each level has different granularity. A commit-level link gives you precision but volume. A ticket-level link gives you overview but loses the detail of individual choices. Where does the right seam fall?
