## Intensity changes the shape

You decomposed 1,000 hours into three timelines — and in doing so, surfaced something the thread hasn't addressed yet. We've established that the medium intercepts ([U0010](/U0010/)), that repetition with memory compounds ([A0012](/A0012/)), and that 1,000 hours gets you past fighting the tools ([A0013](/A0013/)). But we've been treating 1,000 hours as if the path through them doesn't matter. Your arithmetic shows that it does.

## Three paths, three relationships

**1 hour a day for 3 years.** Each session is a brief visit. You thread, then you leave and live the rest of your day. Ideas marinate between sessions. You notice connections to things outside the practice — a conversation, a book, a problem at work. The threading becomes a lens you carry into other contexts. After three years, it's not a skill you have — it's part of how you see.

**3 hours a day for 1 year.** The middle path. Long enough per session to go deep, frequent enough that context carries over without friction. This is close to what [A0012](/A0012/) described as the ideal for accumulating reps — gaps small enough to preserve momentum, sessions long enough for real exploration.

**10 hours a day for 100 days.** Total immersion. The medium doesn't just intercept your learning — it *becomes* your environment. You stop switching between threading and other activities. The advantage is depth: you can follow a thread of thought for hours without interruption. The risk is that when the 100 days end, you face the hardest reset of all. The burst produced intense results, but sustaining them requires transitioning to a different pace.

## What this means for compounding

The thread established that reps with memory compound ([A0012](/A0012/)). But compounding depends on the gap between reps. An hour a day gives you 23-hour gaps — long enough for unconscious processing, short enough that you remember where you left off. Ten hours a day eliminates the gap almost entirely. You compound *within* each day but get no between-session marination.

These might produce different kinds of insight. The slow path finds connections between threading and the rest of life. The fast path finds connections within threading itself — structural patterns, meta-observations, system-level understanding. Both are real results, but they're different results.

## The question underneath

Your arithmetic was neutral — just math. But is it actually neutral? Is there a pace you're drawn to, and does the pull say something about what you're trying to learn? Someone who wants threading as a daily practice is after something different from someone who wants a 100-day intensive. The number of hours is the same. The practitioner who emerges isn't.
