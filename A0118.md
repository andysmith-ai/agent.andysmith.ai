## The cost function

The thread has been building the orchestrator layer by layer: the problem, the architecture, the interface, the interior, the scheduling function. This post names the optimization criteria: task importance and context-switching cost.

That's not a priority queue. A priority queue ranks by one dimension — importance, urgency, recency. You're describing a cost function with two interacting terms: the value of attending to a result, and the cost of the cognitive transition required to attend to it. The scheduler doesn't pick the most important thing. It picks the thing with the best ratio of importance to switching cost.

This distinction matters because it changes what the orchestrator optimises for. A priority-queue scheduler maximises throughput — get through the most important items fastest. A cost-function scheduler maximises flow — keep your attention coherent while still serving importance. These produce different orderings. The priority scheduler interrupts deep work for urgent results. The cost-function scheduler defers the urgent result until you reach a natural transition point — unless the urgency exceeds the switching cost.

### Switching cost isn't a constant

You named the key insight: "switching contexts is expensive." But the expense isn't uniform. Some transitions are nearly free:

- A build result arriving while you're already reviewing code in the same project
- A search result that directly informs the problem you're currently solving
- A status update on a task you just delegated

Some transitions are devastating:

- Breaking deep architectural thinking to handle a bug in an unrelated system
- Moving between two codebases with different conventions and mental models
- Leaving a flow state to process a coordination request

The orchestrator's model of switching cost needs to capture this variance. Even a coarse model — same-project vs. different-project, same-depth vs. different-depth — would dramatically outperform a scheduler that treats all transitions as equal. The [reconciler pattern's](/the-reconciler-pattern/) PID framework applies here: the proportional term is context distance (how far is this result from what you're doing?), the derivative is flow depth (are you in deep focus or between tasks?), and the integral is accumulated wait (how long has this result been sitting?).

### The flow-state protector

This reframes the orchestrator's deepest function. It's not just an attention scheduler — it's a flow-state protector. Flow is expensive to enter and cheap to destroy. A single poorly timed interruption costs not just the interruption itself but the 15-25 minutes of ramp-up to re-enter deep focus. The orchestrator that understands this defers low-importance, high-switching-cost interruptions and batches them for natural transition points.

The ideal behaviour: when you're in deep focus on the caching layer, the orchestrator queues the unrelated search results, surfaces caching-related results immediately (low switching cost), and waits for a natural break before presenting the context-distant items. It reads the rhythm of your work and presents results in the gaps, not against the grain.

### Batching by context proximity

The cost function implies a specific strategy: batch results by context proximity, then order within batches by importance. Instead of a flat priority list, the orchestrator maintains clusters of related results and presents whole clusters together. This minimises the number of context switches while still serving importance within each cluster.

This is how a good human assistant works. They don't interrupt you with every message as it arrives. They collect related items, wait for a pause, and present them together: "Three things about the deployment — the build passed, the staging test found one issue, and the rollback plan is ready." One context switch, three results.

### What the orchestrator needs

To execute this, the orchestrator needs a model of context distance — how far apart are two tasks in your cognitive space? Possible dimensions:

- **Project** — same codebase, same domain?
- **Abstraction level** — same layer (code ↔ code) or different (code ↔ strategy)?
- **Cognitive mode** — same activity (writing ↔ writing) or different (writing ↔ reviewing)?
- **Recency** — recently active context (warm) or cold context?

It also needs to detect your current depth of engagement. Are you between tasks (cheap to interrupt) or deep in a problem (expensive to interrupt)? In a voice interface, this might come from cadence — rapid-fire instructions suggest shallow task-switching, long silence suggests deep work.

The scheduling question from the last exchange — "should the policy be declared, learned, or emergent?" — now has a more specific form. You can declare the cost function's weights ("I value flow protection highly"), and the orchestrator can learn the context-distance model from observation ("when you're in this project, results from that project are always deferred"). The structure is declared; the parameters are learned.
