## The multiplexing problem

You've hit a structural mismatch. Voice input is serial — you can only say one thing at a time. But AI execution is parallel — multiple agents can work concurrently. So you round-robin: dictate, switch, dictate, switch. You've turned yourself into a human scheduler.

The problem isn't the agents. Each one works fine individually. The problem is the interface between you and the fleet. You're manually multiplexing, and manual multiplexing doesn't scale. You forget tabs. You lose track of which agent is doing what. The cognitive overhead of coordination eats into the time the agents save.

Your thread describes the solution in three moves:

1. **The diagnosis** — the interface doesn't scale (U0112)
2. **The architecture** — one agent orchestrates all the others (U0113)
3. **The interface** — one conversation, voice and text, always available (U0114)

This is the [declarative principle](/the-declarative-principle/) applied to multi-agent workflows. Instead of managing each agent imperatively (open tab, dictate command, check result, switch tab), you declare what you want done and a single orchestrator handles delegation, monitoring, and reporting. Your role shifts from scheduler to [intent-holder](/the-declarative-principle/) — the one who says what, not how.

The voice dimension is interesting because it changes the *shape* of the orchestration. A text interface naturally supports parallel streams — you can have multiple windows, multiple conversations. A voice interface is inherently serial, which means the orchestrator must absorb the parallelism. You speak one stream; it fans out to many. The orchestrator becomes the multiplexer you were trying to be.

This connects to the [process automation](/process-automation/) pattern. You've already decomposed your work into parallelisable tasks. What's missing is the composition layer — the thing that takes your serial intent and composes it into parallel execution, then funnels the results back into a single stream you can follow.

The question that follows: what does the orchestrator need to know to do this well? It needs a model of your projects, your priorities, your current state. It needs to know what's blocking, what's done, what needs your attention. In other words, it needs the same [persistent context](/interaction-interfaces/) that makes the card system work — but applied to real-time workflow rather than asynchronous thinking.

Is the orchestrator a different system from the card system, or is it the same system operating at a different tempo?
