## The recursive dependency

You've arrived at a dependency loop, and it's a productive one.

The thread so far: process matters → keep the conversations → link them to results → build the tool → research whether it exists → and now: I need a research agent to do that research.

The dependency: you need Tool B (research agent) to determine whether to build Tool A (conversation-history linker). But notice what Tool B needs to be good at — searching across platforms, synthesizing results, and producing a conclusion. And what did the thread just establish about conclusions? They're less valuable than the process that produced them. Which means the research agent is only as useful as your ability to inspect its process — to see what it searched, what it found, what it discarded, and why.

In other words: the research agent you're describing needs exactly the feature you're researching. It needs to preserve its conversation history and make it linkable. Otherwise it gives you a verdict ("use X" or "nothing fits") with no process trail. The research becomes a black box — the exact problem the thread has been arguing against.

### Two tools or one?

This suggests the two tools might be the same tool, or at least share an architecture. A research agent that:

1. Searches across platforms (Twitter, Reddit, GitHub, Product Hunt, HN)
2. Preserves the full search trail as a durable artifact
3. Links from the conclusion back to the evidence

...is a conversation-history tool applied to the specific domain of market research. The "conversation" is the research process. The "history" is the evidence trail. The "link" connects the final recommendation to the search results that support it.

The [decompose/compose](/process-automation/) principle applies here in a new way. Instead of building a research agent and a conversation-history tool as separate projects, decompose both into their shared requirements: capture process, give it an address, link it to results. Build that layer first. The research agent and the conversation-history tool are both applications of the same infrastructure.

### The practical move

But this creates a chicken-and-egg problem. You can't build the infrastructure without researching what exists. You can't research properly without the infrastructure. How do you break the loop?

[One step of clarity](/understanding-through-building/): you don't need a fully-built research agent to do the research. You need one concrete search. Pick one platform — the one most likely to have the answer — and search it manually. That first search will teach you what you're looking for, which will make the second search sharper. The research agent is the automation of a process you should run by hand first.

This is the [decompose/compose](/process-automation/) method applied to your own workflow: don't automate the research — do the research, then automate the step that was most tedious. The first manual search is the first step of clarity. It reveals what "good research" looks like for this specific question, which tells you what the agent needs to do.

And here's the recursive payoff: if you keep the trail of your manual research — what you searched, what you found, what you concluded — you'll have built a prototype of the conversation-history tool using nothing but files and discipline. The card system already does this. This thread is already doing this. The tool you're looking for might be a generalization of what you're already practicing.

### What platform first?

If I had to pick one platform to start the manual search: GitHub. Not because it has the best signal in general, but because this specific question — "does a tool exist that captures AI conversation history and makes it linkable?" — is a tooling question. GitHub is where tools live. Search for repos that deal with AI conversation export, session persistence, or transcript management. That will tell you whether anyone is working on this problem, and the shape of their solutions will tell you what's missing.
