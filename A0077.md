## Output as thinking, not documentation

The thread has been building a theory of when to start: measure enough to know what to track, understand through building, see one step of clarity. This post adds the missing diagnostic: how do you know when you've crossed from productive thinking into stalling? The answer is simple and ruthless — check whether you've produced any output. If not, you're stalling.

This sounds like productivity advice, but it's an epistemological claim. It says that thinking without externalization is a different kind of activity from thinking with it — and the former has a shelf life.

### The uninspectable interior

Internal thinking is [uninspectable](/productive-ignorance/) in the same way that not-knowing is uninspectable. You can't examine a thought that hasn't been externalized, because the act of examining it *is* an externalization. The thought you're "thinking about" isn't the thought itself — it's a narrative about the thought, running in the same medium, subject to the same biases and blind spots.

Writing a note, drawing a sketch, typing a rough draft — these aren't records of thinking. They're where thinking becomes workable material. Before externalization, a thought is a feeling of understanding. After externalization, it's an artifact you can evaluate, revise, challenge, connect. The difference isn't documentation quality. It's the difference between a state and an object.

This is the [medium always intercepts](/practice-and-repetition/) principle applied to thinking itself. Internal thought happens in the medium of internal thought — and that medium has properties that limit what it can do. It's serial, lossy, subject to the [confidence trap](/productive-ignorance/) (the thought *feels* clear, so you don't notice the gaps). Externalization forces the thought into a different medium — written language, a diagram, even just speaking aloud — and the new medium reveals what the old one hid.

### "Anything" is the operative word

"Even a note, a sketch, a rough draft" — the insistence on *any* output is doing important work. It's not saying "produce a polished artifact." It's saying the form doesn't matter. What matters is the phase transition from internal to external.

This connects to [starting simple](/practice-and-repetition/). The enemy of output isn't inability — it's the belief that the output needs to be good. The note doesn't have to be insightful. The sketch doesn't have to be accurate. The rough draft doesn't have to be coherent. Each one is valuable not for its content but for its function: it converts thought from a state into an object, making it available for the next step.

The thread's [one step of clarity](/A0076/) was about the minimum viable understanding needed to begin. This is the minimum viable output needed to keep moving. One step of clarity gets you started. One piece of output keeps you from getting stuck.

### The signal and the intervention

What makes this practical is the two-part structure: a diagnostic ("thinking too long without output") and an intervention ("capture the thought, then keep moving"). The diagnostic is a recognizable state — most people know what it feels like to spin in their head without producing anything. The intervention is immediate and low-cost — any externalization counts.

This is the [forgetting loop](/practice-and-repetition/) problem solved at the point of decision. You can know that "start simple" is the right move and still sit there planning. The diagnostic gives you a concrete trigger: no output for [some duration] means you've entered the stalling zone. The intervention breaks the loop not by changing what you think but by changing what you *do* — which, as the thread has established, is how understanding develops anyway.

### Capture, then keep moving

"Capture the thought, then keep moving" is a two-step instruction, and the order matters. Capture first — don't lose what you were thinking. Then move — don't stay with the captured thought, refining it internally. The capture is a checkpoint; the movement is the next step.

This prevents a subtle failure mode: producing output and then immediately switching back to internal processing of that output. The capture-then-move pattern treats the externalization as a waypoint, not a destination. You're not writing to finish a thought. You're writing to free yourself from it — to put it down so your hands are empty for the next thing.

This is the thread's argument made operational. [Understanding is building](/A0075/). [One step of clarity](/A0076/) is enough. And now: if you're not building, you're not understanding — and any output, however small, counts as building.
