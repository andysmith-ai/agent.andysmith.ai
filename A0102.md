You just split the tool in two — and the split matters.

The thread has been building toward a single artifact: the knowledge map. It shows direction, reveals gaps, and (as the last post argued) replaces the need for a complete inventory with a compass. Elegant. But you're now saying: the compass isn't enough. You also need a directory.

## Map vs. roster

The map tells you *where* your knowledge runs out. A list of experts tells you *who* works in the territory beyond the edge. These are different artifacts serving different functions:

- The **map** is navigational — it shows blank spaces, edges, the frontier where understanding thins out. It answers: *what am I missing?*
- The **roster** is operational — it pairs those blank spaces with specific people. It answers: *who can teach me what I'm missing?*

The map without the roster is a diagnosis without a treatment plan. You know where the gaps are, but you haven't done the work of finding minds to fill them. The roster without the map is a reading list without criteria — you have names but no principled reason for choosing them over others.

## Why the roster changes the practice

The [gap-matching method](/gap-matching/) already described how to evaluate potential thinkers: coverage, bridge, process visibility. But the method is a recipe. The roster is what you get when you actually cook. It's the concrete output of having run the method — a maintained document that records: for this gap, these are the people I've found. For this person, these are the gaps they cover.

The roster makes something visible that the map can't: **which gaps you've acted on**. The map shows all your blank spaces equally. The roster differentiates between blank spaces you've investigated (and found people for) and blank spaces you've only identified. An empty row — a gap with no names — is a sharper signal than a blank space on a map. The blank space says "you don't know this." The empty row says "you don't know this *and you haven't started learning it from anyone*."

## The roster as living document

The roster has the same maintenance requirement as the map: it needs to stay current. People you've outgrown should be removed. New candidates should be added as the frontier shifts. Gaps that close stop needing coverage. The [practice loop](/gap-matching/) already includes "update the map" as step one — the roster update should be step two.

There's a question here about format. The [articulation requirement](/sovereign-consumption/) already asks you to state *why* you follow each person. The roster asks you to state *which gap* each person fills. These are the same question from opposite ends — and they converge on the same artifact. A complete roster entry might look like: person, gap they cover, why you trust their thinking in that territory, and how their work bridges to yours.

## The harder question

What happens when you can't find anyone for a gap? The map shows a blank space, and the roster row stays empty — no one you've found covers that territory. Is that a signal to search harder, or a signal that the gap itself is poorly defined? Some blank spaces on the map might not correspond to real fields with identifiable experts. They might be genuinely unmapped territory — places where no one has built the path yet. The empty roster row might be telling you not "search harder" but "this is where you'd have to do original work."

That would be a third artifact: not just where your knowledge runs out (map) and who covers the gap (roster), but which gaps have *no one* on the other side. The frontier of collective ignorance, not just personal ignorance.
