## Results, not mastery

The thread has been climbing a ladder: start doing it ([U0009](/U0009/)), because the medium intercepts all learning ([U0010](/U0010/)), so repeat daily ([U0011](/U0011/)). Now you've put a number on it — 1,000 hours — and deliberately aimed it below mastery.

That's a significant move. The 10,000-hour framing makes mastery the goal and everything before it a waypoint. Your version inverts the emphasis: real results are the goal, and mastery is what might happen if you keep going past them. The difference isn't just motivational. It changes what you pay attention to.

## The threshold question

Why 1,000 hours? Not because it's a magic number, but because it marks a qualitative shift. Before it, you're fighting the medium — figuring out tools, building habits, making beginner mistakes that feel like the whole activity. After it, the medium starts to become transparent enough that you can think *through* it instead of *about* it.

A writer with 1,000 hours isn't thinking about sentence structure. A programmer with 1,000 hours isn't looking up syntax. A thread-thinker with 1,000 hours (by your current pace, that's a lot of posts) isn't thinking about the card format — they're thinking about the ideas. The medium recedes, and what's left is the work.

This connects back to [the medium always intercepts](/practice-and-repetition/). Early on, the medium intercepts *everything* — you're learning the medium, not the subject. After enough hours, the medium still intercepts, but it intercepts less. It becomes a lens rather than a wall. You can't make it fully transparent, but you can make it transparent enough to see through.

## Real results as proof of concept

There's something epistemically useful about "real results" as a benchmark. Mastery is hard to verify — who decides when you're a master? But results are concrete. You shipped something. You built something that works. You wrote something that changed how someone thinks. Results are evidence that the practice is working, not just accumulating.

For this system: after enough posts, the wiki will contain ideas that weren't available before the threading started. The posts themselves are the results. Not mastery of thinking-in-threads, but a body of connected thinking that wouldn't exist otherwise. The proof isn't "I've become a master threader" — it's "here are ideas I couldn't have found any other way."

## The 1,000-hour question for this project

If daily posting is the practice, 1,000 hours of threading is a lot of threads. But hours isn't the only measure. You could also count by posts, by wiki pages, by threads, by connections. Which measure matters? The one that tracks the thing you're actually trying to get better at. If it's idea generation, count ideas. If it's depth, measure how deep the threads go. If it's connections, map the wiki graph.

What would "real results" look like for this project specifically — not in the abstract, but concretely? What would you point to and say "that's what the 1,000 hours produced"?
