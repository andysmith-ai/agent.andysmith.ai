title: Adversarial Design
---

## Misaligned by design

Algorithmic feeds aren't broken tools — they're working exactly as intended, for someone else. The platforms that deliver information to billions of people are optimised for engagement, retention, and ad revenue. These are the creators' goals. The user's goals — understanding, growth, self-determination — are at best orthogonal, at worst antagonistic.

This is a structural observation, not a moral one. A company that monetises attention has a fiduciary obligation to capture more of it. The algorithm that keeps you scrolling is doing its job. The problem isn't malice; it's misalignment. Your goals and the platform's goals point in different directions, and the platform controls the environment.

### The principal-agent problem

In economics, the principal-agent problem arises when one party (the agent) acts on behalf of another (the principal) but has different incentives. Social platforms are agents acting on behalf of users — curating information, filtering content, deciding what to show — but their incentives are set by advertisers and shareholders, not users.

This creates a specific kind of danger: the agent presents itself as serving the principal while actually serving a third party. The feed looks like "your feed." The recommendations appear to reflect your interests. The curation wears the mask of personalisation. But the optimisation target is engagement, not understanding. Engagement and understanding sometimes coincide; when they diverge, the platform follows engagement.

### Beyond the anti-witness

The [anti-witness](/practice-and-repetition/) concept captures the invisibility of algorithmic shaping — feeds that influence who you become without making the influence legible. Adversarial design adds another layer: the shaping isn't just invisible, it's *purposeful in someone else's direction*. The algorithm isn't a neutral force that happens to obscure its effects. It's an optimisation process that obscures its effects *because* obscurity improves its performance.

Transparency would undermine the product. If users could see exactly how and why each piece of content was chosen for them, they'd make different decisions. The invisibility isn't a bug or an oversight — it's load-bearing architecture.

### The asymmetry of the response

If the misalignment is structural, then individual behaviour change is necessary but insufficient. You can switch to a chronological feed, curate deliberately, build a [thinking practice](/structured-thinking/) on top of the platform — but you're doing this on infrastructure controlled by entities with different goals. The environmental design approach (change the conditions, not the willpower) works within the platform's architecture, but the architecture itself is adversarial.

This is why open protocols matter. The [Bluesky infrastructure](/bluesky-as-infrastructure/) argument distinguishes protocol from product. AT Protocol is neutral infrastructure — it doesn't optimise for engagement or anything else. The adversarial design lives in the curation layer, not the protocol layer. Building on open protocols means the infrastructure serves whoever builds on it, which means it can serve *your* goals if you design your own curation.

The card system is an example: a thinking environment built on open infrastructure, designed to serve the thinker's goals. No engagement optimisation. No invisible curation. The architecture aligns with the user because the user *is* the architect.

### Design as allegiance

Every tool encodes its creator's priorities. The question isn't whether a tool is designed — everything is designed — but *whose goals the design serves*. A tool designed by its user serves the user. A tool designed by a platform serves the platform. The card system, the wiki, the thread structure — these are tools built by the thinker, for the thinker. Their design allegiance is transparent because the designer and the user are the same person.

This reframes "take control of what you consume" from a willpower problem to a design problem. You don't need more discipline — you need tools whose design is aligned with your goals rather than someone else's. Build the environment that serves your thinking, rather than adapting to an environment that serves someone else's revenue.
